The Science-Policy Gap
Scientists and policymakers read in opposite directions

🔬 How Scientists Present

  1. Background — the literature and context
  2. Methods — how we did it
  3. Results — what we found
  4. Conclusions — what it means

🏛 What Policymakers Want

  1. Results — what did you find?
  2. So what? — why does it matter to me?
  3. Background — only if I need it
The challenge is not relevance. The challenge is translation.

Message Box — Worked Example
Ocean Accounts for the Global Biodiversity Framework

Problems

GBF targets require data on marine ecosystems; most countries lack baselines and monitoring capacity.

Solutions

Compile ocean accounts using SEEA EA + GOAP guidance to provide standardised data.

ISSUE

Ocean accounts for the GBF

Benefits

Meet reporting obligations, attract conservation finance, and inform marine spatial planning.

So What

Without ocean accounts, governments cannot credibly report on GBF progress or access funding.

Tool 2 — The Policy Brief
Know what you are writing — and what you are not

✅ What a Policy Brief IS

  • Short — a 5–10 minute read
  • Jargon-free — plain language throughout
  • Actionable — specific recommendations
  • Evidence-based — grounded in data, not opinions

❌ What It Is NOT

  • A research paper
  • A methods description
  • A literature review
  • An opinion piece
Think of it as a bridge between your data and a decision — not as a summary of your work.

Writing Tips
Translate jargon and follow five core principles

Jargon Translation

“Extent account” ➔ “We mapped X hectares of coral reef”
“Condition index declined” ➔ “Reef health dropped 15% in 5 years”
“Ecosystem services valued at” ➔ “Coastal protection saves $X million/year”
“GVA contribution” ➔ “Ocean industries contribute X% of GDP”

Writing Principles

  • ✓ Lead with what you know — not how you learned it
  • ✓ One or two key numbers — not a full table
  • ✓ No methods or statistical details
  • ✓ Write for someone outside your field
  • ✓ Every sentence answers “so what?”
