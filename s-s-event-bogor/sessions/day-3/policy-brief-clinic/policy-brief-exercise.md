# Policy Brief Exercise Worksheet

**Session:** Ocean Accounting Clinic Pt.2 -- Policy Implications and Brief Development
**Time:** Day 3, 09:10-12:00

Work through Parts 1, 2, and 3 in order during the session. Each part builds on the previous one.

## Part 1: Your Message Box (30 minutes)

### Step 1: Identify your audience

Start here. This should take about 2 minutes.

Do not write "policymakers." Name a specific person, office, or body.

| Question | Your answer |
|----------|-------------|
| Who makes decisions about your ocean area or topic? | |
| Who controls the budget for marine/coastal management in your country? | |
| Who needs this data for reporting (e.g., SDG, NBSAP, NDC focal point)? | |
| **My target reader is:** | |

### Step 2: Fill in your Message Box (18 minutes)

Use the separate Message Box template handout. These prompting questions will help you fill in each section.

**Issue (center):**
- What is the overarching ocean topic you are working on?
- Can you say it in one short phrase (under 10 words)?
- Example: "Tracking coral reef health in Fiji" or "Measuring Indonesia's ocean economy"

**Problems (top):**
- What specific problems does your country or accounting area face?
- What data gaps prevent good decisions from being made?
- What is going wrong that your ocean account can document?

**So What (right):**
- Why should your target reader care about this?
- What happens if nothing changes?
- What is at stake -- economically, socially, environmentally?
- Test: "If a minister read only one sentence from my box, which would it be?"

**Solutions (bottom):**
- What can ocean accounts specifically tell us about this problem?
- What actions do the data support?
- Be specific to your account type. "Ocean accounts show that mangrove loss accelerated by 15% between 2018 and 2023" is better than "ocean accounts provide data."

**Benefits (left):**
- If your recommendations were followed, what would improve?
- Think broadly: direct and indirect benefits
- Who else benefits beyond your target reader?

### Step 3: Pair share (10 minutes)

Swap your Message Box with your neighbor. Read theirs and answer these questions:

1. Can you understand the So What without knowing their technical background?
2. Is the Issue clear in one phrase?
3. Are the Solutions specific enough to act on?
4. Write one suggestion for improvement on their sheet.

## Part 2: Draft your policy brief (40 minutes)

Use the separate policy brief template handout. Work through it section by section, using your completed Message Box as the source material.

### Drafting order and sentence starters

**Section 1: Title (2 minutes)**

Write an action-oriented title that names your country and topic.

Pattern: [Action/Finding]: [How Ocean Accounts Connect to Policy Topic]

Examples:
- "Tracking Mangrove Loss in Vanuatu: Ocean Accounts for Climate Adaptation Planning"
- "Measuring Kenya's Ocean Economy: A Baseline for Blue Economy Strategy"
- "Coral Reef Decline in Samoa: What Condition Accounts Tell Us About MPA Effectiveness"

**Section 2: Context (8 minutes)**

Start with your reader's world, not your methodology. Sentence starters:

- "[Country/region] depends on [ocean resource] for [economic activity, livelihoods, food security]..."
- "The [policy process, e.g., NBSAP revision, blue economy strategy] requires [type of data] that is currently [unavailable, fragmented, outdated]..."
- "Ocean accounts provide a standardized way to [what they offer, in plain language]..."

**Section 3: What the accounts show (15 minutes)**

This is where your Day 2 account results go. Include one key number, trend, or table.

- "Between [opening year] and [closing year], [indicator] changed from [value] to [value], a [percentage] [increase/decline]..."
- "[Account type] results show that [finding in plain language]..."
- Include one simple table or figure if you have it. Strip away everything except the headline result.

**Section 4: Policy implications and recommendations (10 minutes)**

Connect your findings to actions your reader can take. Write 2 to 3 specific recommendations.

- "These findings suggest that [policy implication]..."
- "We recommend that [specific actor] [specific action] by [timeframe or mechanism]..."
- "Ocean account data can be integrated into [specific policy process] to [specific benefit]..."

Test each recommendation: Is it specific? Is it actionable by your target reader? Is it realistic?

**Section 5: Key messages (5 minutes)**

Write these last. Pull 3 to 4 bullets from what you have already written. Each bullet should:
- Stand on its own (readable without context)
- State a fact or recommendation, not a question
- Avoid jargon
- Be scannable in under 10 seconds

### Account-type adaptation table

If you are unsure how your account type connects to policy, use this table:

| Your account type | Policy angle | Example "So What" for your brief |
|------------------|-------------|----------------------------------|
| Extent | Habitat loss tracking for NBSAP/GBF targets | "Mangrove loss of X ha between 2015 and 2023 means Y km of coastline lost natural storm protection" |
| Condition | Ecosystem health trends for MPA effectiveness | "Coral cover in managed areas declined at the same rate as unprotected reefs, raising questions about current MPA management" |
| Services | Valuing ecosystem contributions to livelihoods and GDP | "Coastal ecosystems provide an estimated $X million in storm protection services annually, but this value is declining" |
| OESA | Ocean economy baseline for blue economy strategy | "Ocean industries contribute X% of GDP but monitoring systems cannot track whether growth is sustainable" |
| OTSA | Tourism sustainability and ecosystem dependency | "Coastal tourism employs X people and generates $Y, but depends on ecosystem health that is declining" |
| Waste | Pollution sources and trends for regulation | "Plastic waste entering coastal waters increased X% over 5 years, exceeding safe thresholds in Y% of monitored sites" |
| Social | Governance gaps and community participation | "Only X% of coastal communities participate in MPA planning despite depending on marine resources for their livelihoods" |
