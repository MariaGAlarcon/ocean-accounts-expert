## Slide 2: The science-policy gap

Scientists and policymakers think about information in opposite order.

**How scientists present:**

1. Background and literature
2. Methods
3. Results
4. Conclusions

**What policymakers want:**

1. Results -- what did you find?
2. So what -- why does it matter for my decisions?
3. Background -- only if I need more detail

(Adapted from Baron, 2010. Escape from the Ivory Tower: A Guide to Making Your Science Matter.)

The challenge is not that your research is irrelevant. The challenge is that the way scientists are trained to communicate puts the most important information last. Policy briefs fix this by leading with findings and implications.

## Slide 3: Science is one input among many

Policy decisions are shaped by six elements, not just evidence (Cairney and Heikkila, 2014):

| Element | What it means | Example |
|---------|--------------|---------|
| Policy actors | People and organizations involved in the process | Ministry of Environment, fishing cooperatives, tourism boards |
| Networks | Relationships between actors | Regional fisheries management organizations, GOAP community |
| Institutions | Rules, norms, and traditions | National accounting standards, SEEA framework, budget cycles |
| Ideas | Beliefs and worldviews | "Blue economy" framing, sustainability vs growth priorities |
| Events | Shocks that open policy windows | Coral bleaching event, oil spill, new government, UNOC3 |
| Socioeconomic context | Economic conditions, demographics | Coastal poverty rates, tourism dependency, climate exposure |

Your ocean account results enter this system as evidence. A good policy brief positions your evidence within the elements your audience cares about.

(Evans and Cvitanovic, 2018)

## Slide 4: Know your audience

Different audiences ask different questions about the same information.

| Audience | What they ask |
|----------|--------------|
| Policymakers | Does this support my agenda? Do my constituents care? |
| Managers | What will it cost -- time, effort, money? Who supports this? |
| NGOs | How does this fit our agenda? |
| Scientists | Is it groundbreaking? Is it robust? How does it affect my work? |
| Media | Is it news? Will it sell? Is it a good story? |
| Foundations | Does this fit within our portfolio? |
| Communities | How does this affect safety, health, livelihoods, and natural resources? |

(Baron, 2010)

Before you write anything, name a specific person or office as your reader. "Policymakers" is too vague. Try: "The Director of Fisheries in my country" or "The NBSAP focal point at our Ministry of Environment."

## Slide 5: Tool 1 -- The COMPASS Message Box

The Message Box is a thinking tool that forces you to distil your research into five clear components. It was developed by COMPASS (Communication Partnership for Science and the Sea).

```
+--------------------------------------------------+
|                   PROBLEMS                        |
|         What specific problems exist?             |
|                                                   |
+----------+                        +---------------+
|          |                        |               |
| BENEFITS |        ISSUE           |   SO WHAT     |
|          |   (your overarching    |               |
| What     |    ocean topic)        | Why should    |
| improves |                        | your audience |
| if action|                        | care?         |
| is taken?|                        |               |
+----------+                        +---------------+
|                                                   |
|                   SOLUTIONS                       |
|         What actions do the data support?          |
+--------------------------------------------------+
```

The Issue sits at the center. It is the overarching topic, summarized in a short clear phrase. The four surrounding sections build your case: what is wrong (Problems), why it matters (So What), what can be done (Solutions), and what improves if action is taken (Benefits).

Start with the Issue, then fill in the So What -- it is the hardest and most important section.

## Slide 6: Message Box worked example -- Ocean accounts and the GBF

Here is the GOAP policy brief on the Global Biodiversity Framework, mapped onto a Message Box:

| Section | Content |
|---------|---------|
| **Issue** | Using ocean accounts to track progress on the Global Biodiversity Framework |
| **Problems** | Countries lack standardized data to report on GBF targets. Biodiversity data is fragmented across agencies. Monitoring systems do not connect environmental trends to economic or social outcomes. |
| **So What** | Without consistent measurement, countries cannot track whether the 30x30 target is being met, whether conservation spending is effective, or whether marine ecosystems are improving. The GBF requires reporting that current systems cannot deliver. |
| **Solutions** | Ocean accounts integrate environmental, economic, and social data under the SEEA framework. Extent and condition accounts track habitat and ecosystem health. Services accounts measure benefits. All are aligned with GBF targets 1 through 14. |
| **Benefits** | Countries can monitor NBSAPs with standardized data. Spatial planning becomes evidence-based. Restoration priorities are identified. Conservation effectiveness can be measured over time. Reporting to the CBD becomes consistent and comparable. |

Notice: no methods, no technical jargon, no "we used the AGRRA protocol." The Message Box strips your work down to what matters for the reader.

## Slide 8: Tool 2 -- The policy brief

A policy brief is a short document (2 to 4 pages) that summarizes research for a non-technical audience and identifies policy responses.

What a policy brief is:

- Short: readable in 5 to 10 minutes
- Jargon-free: written for someone outside your field
- Actionable: includes specific recommendations
- Evidence-based: grounded in your data, not opinion

What a policy brief is not:

- A journal article summary
- A methods description
- A literature review
- An advocacy piece for a single position

From Evans and Cvitanovic (2018): a policy brief should "succinctly describe a particular issue, argue why it represents a policy problem, and identify possible policy responses."

Policy briefs work best when a relationship already exists with the target reader. An unsolicited brief sent to someone who has never heard of you is unlikely to be read. Build the relationship first; the brief becomes a tool in that conversation.

## Slide 9: Anatomy of a GOAP policy brief

The existing GOAP policy briefs follow a consistent structure. Here is what each section does:

| Section | Purpose | Length |
|---------|---------|--------|
| **Title** | Action-oriented, names the topic and the connection to ocean accounts | 1 sentence |
| **Key messages** | 4 to 6 bullet points a busy reader can scan in 30 seconds | Half page |
| **Context / Introduction** | Why this topic matters, framed for the target audience | 1 to 2 paragraphs |
| **Ocean accounts contribution** | How ocean accounts address the problem -- the "so what" | 1 to 2 pages |
| **Policy questions or recommendations** | Specific, actionable steps the reader can take | 3 to 5 items |
| **References** | Key sources only (not a full bibliography) | 3 to 5 citations |

Tips for each section:

- **Title:** Can your reader tell the country and topic from the title alone? "Ocean Accounts for Fiji's Coastal Resilience" works. "Policy Brief" alone does not.
- **Key messages:** Write these last, after you know what the brief says. Each bullet should stand on its own.
- **Context:** Lead with the reader's world, not yours. Start with their policy challenge, not your methodology.
- **Recommendations:** Be specific. "Integrate extent account data into the next NBSAP revision" is actionable. "More research is needed" is not.

## Slide 10: Writing tips

**Jargon translation table for ocean accounts:**

| Technical term | Plain language alternative |
|---------------|--------------------------|
| Ecosystem extent account | Tracking the area of habitats like reefs, mangroves, and seagrass |
| Condition index (CI) | A health score from 0 (degraded) to 1 (healthy) |
| SEEA EA framework | The UN's international standard for measuring ecosystems alongside economic data |
| Ecosystem services flow | The benefits nature provides, such as fish, coastal protection, and tourism |
| Reference level | The benchmark for a healthy ecosystem |
| Opening/closing stock | The state of the ecosystem at the start and end of the measurement period |
| Normalization | Converting different measurements to a common 0 to 1 scale |
| OESA | A measure of how much ocean industries contribute to the national economy |

**General writing principles (from Evans and Cvitanovic, 2018; Baron, 2010):**

- Lead with what you know, not what you do not know
- Use data where possible, but keep it simple -- one or two key numbers, not a full results table
- Do not include methods or statistical details
- Use specific examples: "mangrove loss of 200 ha" is stronger than "significant habitat loss"
- Acknowledge your expertise -- you earned it
- Different audiences need different framing of the same data

## Slide 12: Your turn -- Draft your policy brief

**Time:** 40 minutes drafting, then 15 minutes peer review

Use the policy brief template handout. Work through it section by section:

1. **Title and context** (10 min) -- name your country, your topic, and why it matters to your reader
2. **What the accounts show** (15 min) -- your key finding, with one number or table
3. **Recommendations** (10 min) -- 2 to 3 specific actions your reader can take
4. **Key messages** (5 min) -- write these last, after you know what your brief says
5. **Peer review** (15 min) -- swap with a neighbor, use the 8-item checklist

If you did not complete an account on Day 2, choose one of the printed GOAP briefs and reverse-engineer a Message Box from it. This is an excellent learning exercise.

Remember: this is a first draft. The goal is to get your ideas on paper, not to produce a finished document. You can refine it after the event with support from the GOAP Secretariat.
