# Ocean Economy Satellite Accounts -- Clinic Walkthrough Slides

**Time:** 15-30 min depending on participant level
**Purpose:** Guide a fellow through building an Ocean Economy Satellite Account (OESA)

---

## Slide 1: What is an OESA?

An Ocean Economy Satellite Account is an **SNA-based satellite account** that reorganizes existing national accounts data to isolate the ocean economy's contribution. It does not create new data. It reclassifies what national statistical offices already collect.

| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Accounting base** | System of National Accounts (SNA 2008) |
| **What it reorganizes** | Supply-Use Tables, national accounts by industry |
| **What it reports** | Gross output, intermediate consumption, GVA, employment |
| **Compatibility** | GDP-compatible -- results can be compared directly to national GDP |

An OESA answers the question: **how large is the ocean economy, and which sectors drive it?**

---

## Slide 2: The core question

**"What portion of national production of goods and services is related to the ocean?"**

The ocean economy includes 3 components:

| Component | Definition | Examples |
|-----------|-----------|---------|
| **1. On-water production** | Activities that take place on or under the ocean | Fishing, shipping, offshore drilling |
| **2. Coastal-dependent** | Activities that must be located on or near the coast | Port services, coastal tourism, ship building |
| **3. For-ocean-use** | Goods and services produced specifically for ocean activities | Marine equipment, navigation instruments, fishing gear |

Not every dollar earned near the coast is "ocean economy." The OESA applies a clear test: would this activity exist without the ocean?

---

## Slide 3: Six established ocean economy sectors

These 6 sector groups are used by most OESAs worldwide. They provide a common structure for cross-country comparison.

| Sector | Examples | Typical ISIC codes |
|--------|---------|-------------------|
| **Living resources** | Marine fisheries, aquaculture, seaweed harvesting | 0311, 0312, 0321, 0322 |
| **Marine minerals** | Offshore oil and gas, seabed mining, salt extraction | 0610, 0620, 0710, 0899 |
| **Ship and boat building** | Shipyards, boat repair, marine equipment manufacturing | 3011, 3012, 3315 |
| **Marine construction** | Ports, harbours, coastal infrastructure, submarine cables | 4210, 4220, 4290 |
| **Marine transport** | Shipping, port services, maritime logistics, pilotage | 5011, 5012, 5020, 5222 |
| **Coastal tourism and recreation** | Beach tourism, diving, recreational fishing, marinas | 5510, 5520, 7911, 9329 |

Countries may add sectors (marine biotechnology, marine renewable energy, defence) depending on national priorities. The 6 above form the standard starting point.

---

## Slide 4: Core indicators

An OESA produces the same indicators as a standard national account, filtered for the ocean economy.

| Indicator | Formula | What it measures |
|-----------|---------|-----------------|
| **Gross output** | Total value of goods and services produced | Size of ocean production |
| **Intermediate consumption (IC)** | Goods and services used up in production | Cost of inputs |
| **Gross value added (GVA)** | Output - IC | Ocean economy contribution to GDP |
| **GDP contribution** | Sum of GVA across all ocean industries | Total ocean economy size |
| **Employment** | Number of jobs in ocean industries | Ocean economy workforce |
| **Ocean economy share** | Ocean GVA / National GDP x 100 | Relative importance of ocean economy |

**GVA is the headline indicator.** It measures the value the ocean economy adds to the national economy after subtracting the cost of inputs.

---

## Slide 5: The 7-step compilation process

Building an OESA follows 7 steps:

1. **Define scope** -- Set geographic boundaries (EEZ, coastal zones) and economic boundaries (which activities count)
2. **Classify activities** -- Map national industry codes (ISIC/SIC) to ocean economy sectors
3. **Select framework** -- Anchor the account to Supply-Use Tables (SUT) or available industry statistics
4. **Estimate ocean partials** -- Determine what share of each industry is ocean-related
5. **Compile indicators** -- Calculate ocean output, IC, GVA, and employment for each sector
6. **Data integration** -- Reconcile totals, check for double counting, balance the account
7. **Validate results** -- Compare to benchmarks, document assumptions, review with stakeholders

Steps 1-3 are planning. Step 4 is the analytical core. Steps 5-7 produce the account.

---

## Slide 6: The concept of "partials"

The **partial** is the share of a national industry attributed to the ocean economy. This is the most important analytical step.

Some industries are entirely ocean-related (partial = 1.0). Others are mixed -- only a fraction of their output relates to the ocean.

| Industry | Ocean partial | Rationale |
|----------|---:|-----------|
| Fishing | 1.0 | Entirely ocean-based (excluding freshwater) |
| Water transport | 1.0 | All maritime |
| Ship building | 1.0 | All marine vessels |
| Coastal tourism | 0.2 | Only the coastal share of accommodation and food services |
| Marine construction | 0.3 | Ports, harbours, and coastal infrastructure as share of all construction |
| Mining (offshore) | 0.15 | Offshore oil and gas as share of total mining sector |

**How to estimate partials:**
- Direct measurement: use establishment-level data from business registers
- Geographic proxy: share of activity in coastal municipalities
- Administrative records: port authority data, fisheries licenses, tourism surveys
- Expert judgement: informed estimates where data are sparse

Assign a **RAG confidence rating** to each partial: Green (direct evidence), Amber (proxy data), Red (sparse evidence or expert judgement).

## Slide 7: Two compilation pathways

| Feature | Pathway A: Rapid assessment | Pathway B: Full SUT-based |
|---------|---------------------------|--------------------------|
| **Data required** | Industry statistics, administrative records | Supply-Use Tables from NSO |
| **Accounting base** | Sector-by-sector estimates | Balanced SUT framework |
| **Partials** | Applied to available industry data | Applied to SUT rows and columns |
| **Consistency** | Sectors estimated independently | Cross-checked against SUT totals |
| **Time to produce** | 2-4 months | 6-12 months |
| **Best for** | First estimate, advocacy, scoping | Official statistics, policy monitoring |

**Start with Pathway A** if you do not have access to SUTs. Move to Pathway B when SUTs become available or when you need an official statistical product.

## Slide 8: Example output -- South Africa OESA 2018

South Africa compiled a full SUT-based OESA for 2018. Key results:

| Indicator | Value |
|-----------|------:|
| Total ocean GVA | ZAR 132.5 billion |
| Ocean economy share of GDP | 2.74% |
| Ocean economy employment | 316,000 jobs |

**Breakdown by sector:**

| Sector | Share of ocean GVA |
|--------|---:|
| Marine minerals (oil, gas, mining) | 34.7% |
| Marine construction | 30.7% |
| Coastal tourism and recreation | 14.4% |
| Marine transport | 11.9% |
| Living resources (fisheries, aquaculture) | 7.9% |
| Ship and boat building | 0.4% |

Minerals and construction together account for 65% of South Africa's ocean GVA. This reflects the country's offshore oil and gas sector and major port infrastructure investments.

## Slide 9: How OESA relates to SEEA-EA

The OESA and SEEA Ecosystem Accounting (SEEA-EA) are complementary. They measure different things.

| Feature | OESA | SEEA-EA |
|---------|------|---------|
| **Measures** | The economy (market-based GDP contribution) | The environment (ecosystem extent, condition, services) |
| **Units** | Currency (USD, ZAR) and jobs | Hectares, physical units (kg, Mg CO2), and currency |
| **Scope** | Industries that depend on the ocean | Ecosystems that provide benefits to people |
| **Question answered** | What does the ocean contribute to the economy? | What does the economy do to the ocean? |
| **Accounting standard** | SNA satellite account | SEEA EA (UN statistical standard) |

**Together they give the full picture.** An OESA shows that coastal tourism generates USD 200 million in GVA. A SEEA-EA services account shows that coral reef degradation threatens the ecosystem that supports that tourism. One without the other tells only half the story.

## Slide 10: What to do right now

If you want to build an OESA for your country:

1. **Start with the 6 established sectors.** Do not try to cover everything. Use the standard sector groups from Slide 3.
2. **Get your country's SUT or industry statistics.** Contact your National Statistical Office. If SUTs are not available, use published industry data.
3. **Estimate partials for each sector.** Use the methods from Slide 6. Document your sources and confidence levels.
4. **Calculate GVA for each sector.** Ocean GVA = (National output x Partial) - (National IC x Partial).
5. **Sum sectors to get total ocean economy GVA.** Compare to national GDP for the ocean economy share.
6. **Use the exercise sheet to practice.** Work through the SIDS example to build confidence with the calculation.

The exercise provides a worked example with 6 industries, partials, and a full answer key.
