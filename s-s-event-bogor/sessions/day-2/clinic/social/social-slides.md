# Social and Governance Accounts -- Clinic Walkthrough Slides

---

## Slide 1: What Are Social Accounts?

Social accounts compile data on the **social dimensions of the human-ocean relationship**.

- This is **not** SEEA. This is **not** SNA. This is a **new domain** being developed by GOAP.
- The GOAP Technical Guidance Circular provides the conceptual framework, but methodology is still emerging.
- This is **draft guidance**, not an adopted international standard.
- The approach is fundamentally different from quantitative SEEA accounts -- it involves mixed methods (quantitative and qualitative), community co-design, and scoping exercises rather than calculation exercises.

**Key point:** Social accounts are the newest part of ocean accounting. You are at the frontier.

---

## Slide 2: Why Social Accounts Matter

Current ocean accounts (SEEA EA, OESA) tell us about ecosystems and economic activity. They do **not** tell us:

- **Who benefits** from the ocean?
- **Who bears the costs** of degradation or management decisions?
- **What cultural connections** exist between people and the ocean?
- **What livelihoods** depend on the ocean beyond formal employment?
- **Who participates** in ocean governance decisions?

Without this information, policy decisions have blind spots. Governments may protect ecosystems while displacing the communities that depend on them, or grow ocean GDP while deepening inequality.

Social accounts fill this gap.

## Slide 3: Four Pillars of Social Data

| Pillar | What it covers | Example questions |
|---|---|---|
| **Human Wellbeing** | Health, nutrition, life satisfaction | How does ocean proximity affect health outcomes? What is fish consumption per capita? |
| **Ocean-Based Livelihoods** | Employment, income, food security | How many people depend on fishing for their livelihood? What is the gender breakdown of ocean employment? |
| **Equity and Access** | Who gets what, distributional analysis | How are fishing permits distributed across income groups? Who has access to coastal land? |
| **Cultural Connections** | Traditional knowledge, ceremonies, sense of place | What traditional practices connect communities to the ocean? How many hours are spent on ocean-related cultural activities? |

These 4 pillars structure what data you collect and how you organize it.

## Slide 4: Stocks and Flows Framework

Social accounts use the same **stocks and flows** logic as other accounts, adapted for social data.

**Stocks** = social assets and conditions (state at a point in time)

**Flows** = social activities (changes over a period)

| | Stocks (conditions) | Flows (activities) |
|---|---|---|
| **What they measure** | State of social assets at a point in time | Social activities over a period |
| **Examples** | Educational attainment, trust levels, MPA coverage | Hours spent fishing for household consumption, hours volunteering for beach cleanups |
| **Categories** | Human capital, social capital, institutional capital | Unpaid household labour, social transfers, volunteering, cultural/recreational, governance |
| **Unit** | Various (%, count, index) | Primarily **hours** (to align with SNA) |

---

## Slide 5: Example Stock Indicators

### Human capital (stocks)
- Educational attainment of coastal populations
- Fish consumption per capita (kg/year)
- Life satisfaction index for coastal communities
- Proportion of coastal population in poorest income quintile

### Social capital (stocks)
- Trust levels between fishing communities and government
- Number of formal community partnerships
- Participation rate in community resource management groups
- Cross-island or cross-community collaboration agreements

### Institutional capital / governance (stocks)
- MPA coverage as percentage of territorial waters
- Compliance rates with fisheries regulations
- Number of co-management agreements in force
- Customary governance practices recognized in law

## Slide 6: Example Flow Indicators (Measured in Hours)

| Category | Example indicators (hours) |
|---|---|
| **Unpaid household labour** | Hours processing marine catch for household consumption. Hours caring for fishing families. Hours maintaining fishing equipment. |
| **Social transfers** | Hours sharing boats and equipment between households. Hours sharing traditional ecological knowledge. Hours providing informal financial support within fishing communities. |
| **Volunteering** | Hours on beach cleanups. Hours on community coastal monitoring. Hours on volunteer mangrove restoration. |
| **Cultural/recreational** | Hours on traditional ocean ceremonies. Hours on ocean-based recreation (surfing, swimming). Hours on traditional boat building. |
| **Governance participation** | Hours in fisheries management meetings. Hours participating in coastal zone planning. Hours in traditional governance processes. |

**Why hours?** Measuring flows in hours aligns with SNA time-use accounting and allows comparison across activity types.

## Slide 7: Two Development Tracks

There are 2 tracks for developing social and governance accounts:

### Social Account Development -- 9 stages
Starting point: co-design with communities and stakeholders.
Scope: broad -- covers all 4 pillars of social data.

### Governance Account Development -- 6 stages
Starting point: policy context discussion with end-users.
Scope: focused -- covers institutional capital and governance participation.

### Decision point
- Do you already have governance data (MPA records, fisheries regulations, compliance data)?
  - **Yes** --> Start with Governance Account Development (6 stages). It is more focused and can deliver results faster.
  - **No** --> Start with Social Account Development (9 stages). It will help you scope what data exists and what is missing.

---

## Slide 8: The 9 Stages of Social Account Development

| Stage | Name | Key activity |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Co-design** | Meet stakeholders, agree scope, identify policy questions |
| 2 | **Social data audit** | Inventory all existing social data sources |
| 3 | **Access data** | Contact data holders, negotiate access, assess quality |
| 4 | **Consultation** | Present findings to stakeholders, validate indicators |
| 5 | **Gap filling** | Design and conduct primary data collection |
| 6 | **Validation** | Take findings back to communities to sense-check |
| 7 | **Compilation** | Build pilot social account using available data |
| 8 | **Revision** | Integrate primary data, revise and improve |
| 9 | **Decision-making** | Train end-users, integrate into policy cycle |

**Critical point:** The process starts with community engagement (stage 1), not data collection (stage 2). This is by design. Social accounts must reflect what matters to the people they describe.

---

## Slide 9: How Social Accounts Connect to Other Account Types

| Account type | What it tells you |
|---|---|
| **SEEA EA** (ecosystem accounts) | Ecosystem health -- extent, condition, services |
| **OESA** (ocean economy satellite account) | Ocean GDP -- economic contribution of ocean industries |
| **Social accounts** | Who benefits, who is affected, what non-economic values exist |

Together they provide the full picture for equitable ocean governance:

- SEEA EA: Is the ecosystem healthy?
- OESA: Is the ocean economy growing?
- Social accounts: Is that growth reaching the right people? Are cultural values being protected? Are governance processes inclusive?

No single account type answers all the questions. Social accounts complete the picture.

## Slide 10: What To Do Right Now

This is an emerging field. There is no standard template to fill in. But there are concrete steps you can take:

1. **Read the GOAP Technical Guidance Circular** on social and governance accounts. It provides the conceptual framework.

2. **Do the scoping exercise** provided in this clinic. It walks you through identifying what social data already exists in your country.

3. **Talk to Becca at the clinic** about social dimensions relevant to your accounting area.

4. **Consider piloting a social data audit** (stage 2) for your accounting area. This is the most practical first step -- simply cataloguing what social data exists, who holds it, and what gaps remain.

5. **Identify your priority policy question.** Social accounts are driven by policy needs, not by data availability. Start with the question, then find the data.

You do not need to wait for a finalized standard to begin. The scoping work you do now will feed directly into any future standardized framework.
