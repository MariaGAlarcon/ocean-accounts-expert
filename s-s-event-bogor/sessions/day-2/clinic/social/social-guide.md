# Social and Governance Accounts -- Step-by-Step Guide

> **Note:** Social accounts are an emerging domain. The GOAP Technical Guidance Circular provides the conceptual framework, but no standardized table format or calculation methodology has been adopted yet. This guide focuses on **how to scope, design, and compile a pilot social account**. Unlike SEEA accounts, there are no calculation steps -- the process centres on co-design, data auditing, consultation, and compilation using mixed methods (quantitative and qualitative).

---

## Part A: Social Account Development (9 Stages)

### Stage 1: Co-design

**Purpose:** Tailor the social account to local context and policy needs.

**What to do:**
- Convene a meeting with local stakeholders: community representatives, government agencies (fisheries, environment, social welfare, statistics), researchers, and civil society.
- Identify the policy questions the social account should answer.
- Agree on the geographic scope (accounting area): country, province, coastal district, MPA, or island group.
- Select priority indicators from the 4 pillars (human wellbeing, ocean-based livelihoods, equity and access, cultural connections).
- Agree on a timeline and division of responsibilities.

**Example policy questions:**
- How do fishing communities benefit from MPAs?
- What livelihoods depend on the reef system?
- How are ocean resources distributed across income groups?
- What cultural practices are connected to the marine environment?
- Who participates in ocean governance decisions, and who is excluded?

**Output:** Agreed scope document with priority indicators, accounting area, and stakeholder roles.

### Stage 2: Social Data Audit

**Purpose:** Find out what social data already exists in your country.

### Stage 3: Access Data

**Purpose:** Obtain the datasets identified in stage 2 and organize them.

**What to do:**
- Contact each data holder identified in the audit.
- Negotiate data access agreements where needed (some data may be restricted or require formal requests).
- Assess data quality: Is the data complete? Is it recent enough? Does it cover your accounting area?
- Begin organizing data by the stocks/flows framework:
  - **Stocks:** Human capital indicators, social capital indicators, institutional capital indicators.
  - **Flows:** Unpaid household labour (hours), social transfers (hours), volunteering (hours), cultural/recreational activities (hours), governance participation (hours).
- Map data spatially to your accounting area and BSUs where possible.

**Output:** Organized dataset with data classified by stocks and flows, with quality notes.

---

### Stage 4: Consultation

**Purpose:** Validate findings with stakeholders and identify missing perspectives.

**What to do:**
- Present the initial data audit findings to the stakeholder group convened in stage 1.
- Ask: Are these the right indicators? Do they capture what matters?
- Identify gaps: What important social dimensions are missing from the available data?
- Ensure Indigenous and local knowledge holders are included in the consultation.
- Record feedback systematically.

**Key questions for consultation:**
- Does this data reflect the reality of your community?
- What is missing?
- What social changes have you observed that the data does not capture?
- What cultural connections to the ocean are not represented?

**Output:** Validated indicator list, identified gaps, and stakeholder feedback record.

---

### Stage 5: Gap Filling

**Purpose:** Collect primary data for gaps identified in stages 2-4.

**What to do:**
- Design primary data collection instruments for the most critical gaps.
- Methods to consider:
  - **Household surveys** -- for livelihoods, income, food security data.
  - **Time-use surveys** -- for flow data measured in hours.
  - **Focus groups** -- for qualitative data on wellbeing, cultural connections, equity.
  - **Key informant interviews** -- for governance data, institutional knowledge.
  - **Participatory mapping** -- for spatial data on resource use, cultural sites.
- Ensure ethical protocols are followed: free, prior, and informed consent (FPIC) for Indigenous communities; data sovereignty considerations; community ownership of traditional knowledge data.

**Output:** Primary data collection plan with instruments, sampling strategy, and ethical approvals.

---

### Stage 6: Validation

**Purpose:** Take preliminary findings back to communities for sense-checking.

**What to do:**
- Present preliminary results (from secondary data and any initial primary data) to communities and stakeholders.
- Ask: Does this reflect your reality? What is wrong? What is missing?
- Revise indicators and data based on feedback.
- This is not a one-time step -- validation should be iterative.

**Output:** Validated preliminary findings with community feedback incorporated.

---

### Stage 7: Compilation

**Purpose:** Build the first pilot social account using available data.

**What to do:**
- Organize all available data into a pilot social account structure:
  - **Stock tables:** Human capital indicators, social capital indicators, institutional capital/governance indicators. Reported for a reference year.
  - **Flow tables:** Social activities by category (unpaid household labour, social transfers, volunteering, cultural/recreational, governance). Reported in hours for a reference period.
- Map indicators spatially to BSUs where possible.
- Include qualitative findings alongside quantitative data -- narrative descriptions of cultural connections, governance processes, and community perspectives are part of the account.
- Document data sources, methods, and limitations for each indicator.

**Output:** Pilot social account with stock tables, flow tables, qualitative findings, and metadata.

---

### Stage 8: Revision

**Purpose:** Improve the pilot account with primary data and stakeholder feedback.

**What to do:**
- Integrate primary data collected in stage 5.
- Revise indicators based on validation feedback from stage 6.
- Improve spatial resolution where new data allows.
- Update narrative descriptions.
- Present revised account to stakeholders for final review.

**Output:** Revised social account with primary data integrated and validated.

---

### Stage 9: Decision-making

**Purpose:** Ensure the social account is used in policy and governance.

**What to do:**
- Train end-users (government officials, planners, community leaders) on how to interpret the account.
- Develop policy briefs or dashboards that translate account findings into actionable information.
- Integrate the social account into the policy cycle: spatial planning, MPA management, fisheries policy, poverty reduction strategies.
- Establish an update schedule so the account remains current.
- Identify institutional home for ongoing social account maintenance.

**Output:** Trained end-users, policy products, and an institutional arrangement for ongoing updates.

## Part B: Governance Account Development (6 Stages)

Governance accounts are a subset of social accounts, focused specifically on **institutional capital** and **governance participation**. If you already have governance data (MPA records, fisheries regulations, compliance data), this is a more focused starting point.

### Stage 1: Policy Context Discussion

- Initial engagement with end-users (government agencies, coastal managers, MPA authorities).
- Identify the governance questions the account should answer.
- Examples: What proportion of coastal waters are under formal management? What is the compliance rate with fisheries regulations? How many co-management agreements are in force?

### Stage 2: Collect Data

- Gather governance-related data: regulations, spatial management boundaries, compliance records, enforcement data, co-management agreements, MPA management plans.
- Sources: environment ministry, fisheries ministry, Protected Planet, national gazette, local government records.

### Stage 3: Analysis

- Conduct analysis to answer the policy questions identified in stage 1.
- Examples: spatial coverage of governance arrangements, overlap analysis between management zones, compliance trends over time, gap analysis for unmanaged areas.

### Stage 4: Validation

- Present findings to decision-makers, ideally via a dashboard or spatial visualization.
- Ask: Is this accurate? Does it reflect the current governance landscape?
- Identify errors or outdated information.

### Stage 5: Revision

- Make changes based on validation feedback.
- Update data where corrections are identified.
- Refine analysis and visualizations.

### Stage 6: Decision-making

- Finalize governance account products.
- Train end-users on interpretation and use.
- Develop policy products (maps, dashboards, briefs).
- Establish update schedule.

---

## Choosing Your Track

| Question | If yes | If no |
|---|---|---|
| Do you have governance data (MPA records, fisheries regulations, compliance data)? | Start with **Governance Account Development** (6 stages). It is more focused and can deliver results faster. | Start with **Social Account Development** (9 stages). The data audit in stage 2 will help you identify what exists. |
| Do you have a specific governance policy question? | Governance track. | Social track -- the co-design process will help you find the right question. |
| Do you have community engagement capacity? | Social track -- it requires sustained community involvement. | Governance track -- it can be done primarily with institutional data. |

Both tracks can run in parallel if resources allow. The governance account can eventually be integrated as the institutional capital component of a broader social account.
