# Ocean Waste and Emissions Exercise

**Time:** 20-30 minutes
**What you need:** Calculator or Excel, this sheet

---

## Scenario

You are building waste and emissions accounts for a coastal country. Government agencies have provided the following data.

### Water emissions data

| Parameter | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| Total national wastewater discharge | 500,000 tonnes/yr of substances |
| Coastal municipalities share of national wastewater | 35% |
| Treatment rate for coastal sewerage | 60% (meaning 40% is discharged untreated to sea) |
| Coastal wastewater volume | 200 million m3/yr |
| Nitrogen concentration in untreated wastewater | 15 mg/L |
| Phosphorus concentration in untreated wastewater | 3 mg/L |

### Solid waste data

| Parameter | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| National solid waste generation | 2,000,000 tonnes/yr |
| Mismanaged waste rate (uncollected or improperly disposed) | 25% |
| Population within 50 km of coast (share of national) | 40% |
| Marine leakage rate of coastal mismanaged waste | 15% |

### Air emissions data

| Parameter | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| Fishing fleet fuel consumption | 50,000 tonnes/yr |
| Domestic shipping fuel consumption | 200,000 tonnes/yr |
| CO2 emission factor for marine fuel | 3.2 tonnes CO2 per tonne fuel |

---

## Part 1: Calculate nutrient load to sea

Untreated coastal wastewater is discharged directly to the marine environment. Calculate the nutrient load.

**Formula:** Load (tonnes/yr) = Volume (m3/yr) x Untreated share x Concentration (mg/L) x 0.001

**Nitrogen:**
- 200,000,000 m3/yr x 0.40 x 15 mg/L x 0.001 = _______ tonnes N/yr

**Phosphorus:**
- 200,000,000 m3/yr x 0.40 x 3 mg/L x 0.001 = _______ tonnes P/yr

---

## Part 2: Calculate solid waste entering the sea

Use the land-to-sea pathway: national waste, reduced to the mismanaged fraction, then the coastal share, then the marine leakage rate.

**Formula:** Marine waste = National waste x Mismanaged rate x Coastal share x Marine leakage rate

- 2,000,000 x 0.25 x 0.40 x 0.15 = _______ tonnes/yr

---

## Part 3: Calculate air emissions from ocean industries

**Formula:** Emissions = Total fuel consumption x Emission factor

- Fishing fleet: 50,000 x 3.2 = _______ tonnes CO2/yr
- Domestic shipping: 200,000 x 3.2 = _______ tonnes CO2/yr
- **Total ocean industry CO2:** _______ tonnes CO2/yr

---

## Part 4: Build the summary waste account table

Fill in the results from Parts 1-3:

| Waste/emissions type | Flow to marine environment | Unit |
|---------------------|---:|------|
| Nitrogen discharge to sea | _______ | tonnes N/yr |
| Phosphorus discharge to sea | _______ | tonnes P/yr |
| Solid waste entering the sea | _______ | tonnes/yr |
| CO2 from fishing fleet | _______ | tonnes CO2/yr |
| CO2 from domestic shipping | _______ | tonnes CO2/yr |
| **Total CO2 from ocean industries** | _______ | tonnes CO2/yr |

---

## Part 5: Interpret the results

Answer these questions:

1. Which pollution type represents the largest mass flow to the marine environment?

2. Nutrient pollution causes eutrophication (algal blooms, dead zones). What policy actions could reduce nitrogen and phosphorus loads to the sea?

3. The solid waste estimate uses a chain of assumptions (mismanaged rate, coastal share, leakage rate). If the mismanaged waste rate were reduced from 25% to 15% through improved waste collection, how much would marine waste decrease?

4. Domestic shipping produces far more CO2 than the fishing fleet. What does this tell you about where to target emission reduction policies?

5. What additional data would improve these accounts?

---

## Answer Key

### Part 1: Nutrient loads

**Nitrogen:**
- 200,000,000 x 0.40 x 15 x 0.001 = 1,200 tonnes N/yr

**Phosphorus:**
- 200,000,000 x 0.40 x 3 x 0.001 = 240 tonnes P/yr

### Part 2: Solid waste to sea

- 2,000,000 x 0.25 x 0.40 x 0.15 = 30,000 tonnes/yr

### Part 3: Air emissions

- Fishing fleet: 50,000 x 3.2 = 160,000 tonnes CO2/yr
- Domestic shipping: 200,000 x 3.2 = 640,000 tonnes CO2/yr
- **Total:** 800,000 tonnes CO2/yr

### Part 4: Summary table

| Waste/emissions type | Flow to marine environment | Unit |
|---------------------|---:|------|
| Nitrogen discharge to sea | 1,200 | tonnes N/yr |
| Phosphorus discharge to sea | 240 | tonnes P/yr |
| Solid waste entering the sea | 30,000 | tonnes/yr |
| CO2 from fishing fleet | 160,000 | tonnes CO2/yr |
| CO2 from domestic shipping | 640,000 | tonnes CO2/yr |
| **Total CO2 from ocean industries** | **800,000** | tonnes CO2/yr |

### Part 5: Interpretation

1. By mass, air emissions dominate: 800,000 tonnes CO2/yr compared with 30,000 tonnes of solid waste and roughly 1,440 tonnes of nutrients. However, mass alone does not determine environmental impact. Even small quantities of nutrients can cause severe eutrophication, and plastic waste persists for centuries. Each type of pollution requires separate policy attention.

2. To reduce nutrient loads: increase coastal sewerage treatment coverage (the current 60% treatment rate means 40% goes untreated); upgrade treatment plants to secondary or tertiary treatment; reduce fertilizer runoff through buffer zones and better agricultural practices; enforce industrial discharge permits.

3. If mismanaged waste drops from 25% to 15%:
   - New calculation: 2,000,000 x 0.15 x 0.40 x 0.15 = 18,000 tonnes/yr
   - Reduction: 30,000 - 18,000 = 12,000 tonnes/yr (40% decrease)
   - Improving waste collection is one of the most effective interventions for reducing marine debris.

4. Domestic shipping produces 640,000 tonnes CO2/yr versus 160,000 from fishing. Emission reduction policies for the maritime sector (fuel efficiency standards, speed limits, alternative fuels) would have 4 times the impact of targeting the fishing fleet alone. However, both sectors should be addressed.

5. Additional data that would improve these accounts:
   - Substance-specific waste composition (especially plastic fraction)
   - Facility-level discharge monitoring data
   - Actual marine leakage rates based on local studies rather than global estimates
   - Microplastic loads (not captured in standard waste statistics)
   - Sea-based waste sources (shipping, fishing gear loss)
   - Other air pollutants beyond CO2 (SO2, NOx, particulate matter)
