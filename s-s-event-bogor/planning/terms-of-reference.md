# **SOUTH-SOUTH OCEAN ACCOUNTING EXCHANGE 2026**

## **1\. Background**

Ocean accounts are a framework to integrate ocean data, concerning the environment, economy, society and relationships between them \- aligned with UN statistical standards. The ocean accounts extend international standards such as the System of National Accounts (SNA) and the System of Environmental-Economic Accounting (SEEA), enhancing the consistency, comparability and coherence of ocean-related maps, statistics, and indicators. Ocean accounting serves as a way to track ocean assets and flows, link them to economic and social benefits, and support integrated assessment of sustainable ocean economies to inform marine spatial planning, blue carbon management, fisheries, and coastal development.

In recent years, ocean accounting has gained particular momentum in the Global South. The Global Ocean Accounts Partnership (GOAP) and regional development partners have supported pilot projects across:

* Asia-Pacific: Indonesia (national program), Thailand (Phang-Nga Bay), Maldives (Laamu Atoll), Viet Nam (Quang Ninh), and Fiji.   
* Africa: Kenya, Mozambique, Madagascar and South Africa, with coordination supported by IOC Sub Commission for Africa & the Adjacent Island States  
* Latin America and the Caribbean: several countries (e.g. Colombia, Costa Rica, Belize, Barbados, Ecuador, Brazil and Peru) are developing ocean ecosystems and ocean economy accounts, supported by emerging regional initiatives and non-governmental partners. 

Further, South–South cooperation programmes led by the UN Economic and Social Commission for Asia and the Pacific (UNESCAP) and GOAP have further developed common technical guidance for ocean-related SDG statistics through joint pilots and peer learning between Asian, Pacific, African and Small Islands Developing States (SIDS) partners.

The 3rd United Nations Ocean Conference (UNOC3) in Nice in June 2025 marked a turning point by elevating ocean accounting from a technical innovation to a global policy priority. The UNOC3 Political Declaration, “Our ocean, our future: united for urgent action” explicitly recognises “the critical need for national ocean accounting … to inform policy decisions, development planning, integrated coastal zone management, and conservation planning”.[^1] UNOC3 also saw the launch of the Pledge to Advance Ocean Accounts by 2030, led by Costa Rica and supported by the GOAP, with 19 countries and seven organisations committing to integrate ocean accounts into decision-making.[^2] 

Despite the high-level commitments made at UNOC3 and other international forums, countries face significant barriers to implementing ocean accounts. Capacity gaps impose limits to developing countries, with many SIDS and Least Developed Countries (LDCs) constrained due to limited institutional and human resources capacity. Therefore, the Global South requires tailored technical assistance that respects regional contexts, builds on existing knowledge systems, and fosters South-South cooperation. In this case, capacity building with a focus on SIDS, LDCs, and Early Career Ocean Professionals, is essential for ensuring that ocean accounting becomes a practical tool for evidence-based decision-making.

The South-South Ocean Accounting Exchange 2026 is designed to respond to this implementation gap. Hosted at IPB University in Bogor, Indonesia, the Exchange will convene early-career professionals, academic/technical experts, and government stakeholders from Global South countries for three days of hands-on learning, peer exchange, and collaborative problem-solving. Participants will bring real datasets and policy questions from their own countries, work with GOAP and partners to compile pilot ocean accounts, and co-develop policy briefs and baseline indicators aligned with post-UNOC3 commitments, the SDGs, NDCs, and NBSAPs. In parallel, country representatives have the opportunity to engage in closed-door sessions to further regional cooperation frameworks, institutional arrangements and investment pathways for scaling ocean accounting.

## **2\. Purpose**

The South-South Ocean Accounting Exchange 2026 aims to:

1. Support translation of UNOC3 and other high-level commitments on sustainable ocean economies into technical implementation.

2. Support countries to compile accounts that can serve as baselines for tracking progress under SDG 14, related SDGs, NDCs, NBSAPs and regional ocean frameworks.

3. Showcase successful implementations and methods: highlight case studies from Indonesia, Maldives, African coastal states, and Pacific Islands.

4. Strengthen peer-learning networks and South–South cooperation: build and connect practitioners and policymakers across Asia-Pacific, Africa, SIDS and Latin America.

## **3\. Expected outcomes**

By the end of the Exchange, the following outcomes are expected:

1. Ocean accounts pilots (using global and/or participant-contributed data) and a brief for their country or region.

2. Working notes and technical guidance drafts on applying the ocean accounts and SEEA in coastal and marine contexts.

3. Updated implementation for the Indonesia Ocean Accounts Task Force detailing priority sectors, datasets, institutional arrangements and capacity-building needs.

## **4\. Scope**

The Exchange is primarily a technical, academic forum hosted within a university setting, which focuses on:

* Methodological exchange and hands-on compilation of ocean accounts.

* Capacity building for early-career professionals from Global South countries.

* Peer learning across sectors and regions.

In parallel, the program will include separate, invite-only government engagement sessions hosted at a nearby hotel venue. These sessions will focus on (1) Indonesia’s progress in Ocean Accounting and (2) country representatives from South and South-east Asia to focus on:

* Confidential discussions on national implementation status, priorities and challenges within Indonesia.

* South and South-east Asian coordination on regional ocean accounting frameworks and integration into SDGs, NDCs, NBSAPs and ocean policies.

The Exchange will cover both tracks, however, it is not a negotiation or political conference.
