**11:25–11:55 | Part 1: Account Structure and Framework Design**

**Lead:** Mitch (facilitated instruction)

**Format:** Interactive presentation with immediate hands-on application

**Purpose:**

* Teach principles of designing account structure appropriate for context  
* Guide participants through designing their own account structures  
* Emphasize matching structure to policy questions and data availability

**Content:**

**Account Structure Principles (30 minutes \- presentation)**

**Mitch explains:**

**SEEA-EA Framework Refresher:**

* Core structure: **Extent → Condition → Services → Benefits**  
* Spatial and temporal dimensions  
* Accounting identity: Opening stock \+ Changes \= Closing stock  
* Linking accounts: How condition affects services, services provide benefits

**Designing Structure for Your Context:**

* Start with policy questions, not data availability:  
  * What decisions will accounts inform?  
  * What indicators do decision-makers need?  
  * What time frame is relevant (baseline year, multi-year trends)?  
* Then assess data:  
  * What ecosystems can you map?  
  * What condition indicators are measurable?  
  * What services are quantifiable?  
  * What economic data exist?  
* Match structure to feasibility:  
  * Better to have simple accounts with good data than complex accounts with data gaps  
  * Clearly document what's included and excluded, and why  
  * Accounts can be expanded over time as data improve

**Selecting Components:**

* **Ecosystem Types:** Which marine/coastal ecosystems? (mangroves, coral reefs, seagrass, open ocean, coastal wetlands, estuaries)  
* **Spatial Units:** National? Provincial? Protected area? Coastal zone? Grid cells?  
* **Temporal Resolution:** Baseline year? Annual updates? 5-year intervals?  
* **Indicators:**  
  * Extent: Area (hectares, km²), composition (species, habitat types)  
  * Condition: Health indices, biodiversity, water quality, structural integrity  
  * Services: Carbon sequestration, fisheries provisioning, coastal protection, tourism recreation, water filtration  
  * Benefits: Monetary values, employment, food security, cultural values

**Common Pitfalls to Avoid:**

* Over-ambitious scope (trying to account for all ecosystems and services at once)  
* Letting data availability dictate priorities (ignoring policy needs)  
* Insufficient documentation (why choices were made)  
* Ignoring data limitations (presenting incomplete accounts as complete)

**Mitch demonstrates on screen:**

**Example Context:** Coastal province prioritizing mangrove conservation for blue carbon and coastal protection

**Step 1: Define policy questions:**

* How much carbon is stored in provincial mangroves?  
* How has mangrove area changed over 10 years?  
* What coastal protection value do mangroves provide?  
* Where should restoration efforts focus?

**Step 2: Design account structure:**

**Extent Account:**

* Ecosystem: Mangrove forests  
* Spatial unit: Provincial boundary, with district breakdown  
* Temporal: Baseline 2015, updates 2020, 2025  
* Metrics: Total area (hectares), change (gain/loss)

**Condition Account:**

* Indicators: Canopy cover (%), structural complexity, species diversity  
* Spatial unit: Sample sites across districts  
* Temporal: Same as extent (2015, 2020, 2025\)

**Services Account:**

* Carbon sequestration: Tons CO₂ sequestered annually  
* Coastal protection: Kilometers of coastline protected, storm damage avoided  
* Fisheries: Nursery function supporting fish stocks

**Benefits Account:**

* Carbon value: Market value at carbon price  
* Protection value: Replacement cost of seawalls, damage costs avoided  
* Fisheries value: Economic contribution of mangrove-associated fisheries

**Content:**

**Data Requirements and Sources (5 minutes \- presentation)**

**Mitch explains:**

**Data Needs by Account Type:**

**Extent Accounts:**

* Spatial data: Shapefiles, GeoTIFFs, KML files showing ecosystem boundaries  
* Sources: National mapping agencies, satellite imagery (Landsat, Sentinel), aerial surveys, field GPS mapping, global datasets (Global Mangrove Watch, Allen Coral Atlas)  
* Format requirements: Coordinate system documented, attribute tables complete, topology clean (no gaps or overlaps)

**Condition Accounts:**

* Monitoring data: Field surveys, ecological assessments, water quality measurements, biodiversity indices  
* Sources: Research institutions, government monitoring programs, NGO projects, citizen science  
* Format: Time series data with consistent methods, spatial reference, measurement units documented

**Services Accounts:**

* Biophysical models: Carbon stock equations, hydrological models, fisheries productivity relationships  
* Literature values: Published coefficients, benefit transfer databases  
* Sources: Scientific literature, IPCC guidelines, regional studies, expert judgment  
* Format: Clear documentation of assumptions, uncertainty ranges

**Benefits Accounts:**

* Economic data: Market prices, valuation studies, national statistics, household surveys  
* Sources: National statistics offices, tourism boards, fisheries departments, valuation studies  
* Format: Monetary values with year and currency, inflation adjustments if multi-year

**Data Quality Considerations:**

* **Completeness:** Spatial and temporal coverage, missing data documented  
* **Accuracy:** Measurement error, precision, validation against ground truth  
* **Consistency:** Comparable methods over time and across space  
* **Timeliness:** Data currency, update frequency  
* **Documentation:** Metadata standards (sources, methods, dates, limitations)

**Working with Incomplete Data:**

* Acknowledge and document gaps \- don't hide limitations  
* Use best available data and flag uncertainties  
* Prioritize gap-filling for most policy-relevant components  
* Tiered approach: Core accounts with basic data, expanded accounts as data improve  
* Scenario analysis: Show sensitivity to data uncertainties

**Q\&A and Troubleshooting (15 minutes)**

**Mitch opens floor for questions:**

**Expected Questions and Answers:**

**Q: What if my data is incomplete (e.g., only extent, no condition)?**

* A: Platform can generate accounts with extent only. Dashboard will show spatial distribution and change over time. Policy brief will focus on what data allow, and recommend priorities for additional data collection.

**Q: How accurate is the AI-generated text in policy briefs?**

* A: AI drafts text based on data patterns and standard SEEA-EA language. Users MUST review and refine for context accuracy, local terminology, and policy relevance. Think of AI as first draft assistant, not final author.

**Q: Can I use the platform after the Exchange?**

* A: Yes. GOAP will provide access to CoP members. Training webinars will be scheduled. Technical support available via email.

**Q: What if I don't have my data ready until Day 3?**

* A: Submit by end of Day 2 if possible for Day 3 morning outputs. Late submissions will be processed post-Exchange and shared with you.

**Q: Can the platform handle multiple ecosystem types (e.g., mangroves \+ coral reefs)?**

* A: Yes. Upload separate datasets for each ecosystem. Platform generates separate accounts and combined dashboard.

**Q: What file formats are required?**

* A: Spatial: Shapefiles (zipped), GeoTIFF, KML. Tabular: CSV, Excel. Platform auto-detects formats.

**Q: How do I handle data in different coordinate systems?**

* A: Platform can reproject, but you must specify correct source coordinate system. Best practice: standardize to WGS84 before upload.

**Q: What if processing fails or produces errors?**

* A: Tech support volunteers and Mitch available to troubleshoot. Common issues: Coordinate system mismatch, column headers not recognized, file corruption. Most are quickly fixable.

**Q: Can I download the accounts and dashboards to share with my government?**

* A: Yes. All outputs downloadable: Excel tables, dashboard as interactive HTML, policy brief as PDF/Word.

**Q: Does the AI ensure data privacy/security?**

* A: Platform hosted on secure servers. Data visible only to uploader and GOAP support staff (for troubleshooting). Not publicly accessible. Users control who they share outputs with.
