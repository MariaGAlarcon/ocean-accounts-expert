**SEEA-EA Framework Overview (10 minutes)**

* Extension of SNA (System of National Accounts) to environmental domains

* Alignment with UN statistical standards for consistency and comparability

**Ocean Accounts Architecture (15 minutes)**

* **Extent Accounts:** Mapping and measuring ocean/coastal ecosystem areas (mangroves, seagrass, coral reefs, open ocean)  
* **Condition Accounts:** Measuring ecosystem health, biodiversity, water quality  
* **Ecosystem Services Accounts:** Quantifying services (carbon sequestration, coastal protection, fish provisioning, tourism recreation)  
* **Economic Benefits:** Valuing services in monetary terms, linking to national accounts

**Policy Applications (10 minutes)**

* How ocean accounts inform SDG 14 indicators (life below water)  
* Linkages to NDCs (Nationally Determined Contributions) for climate commitments  
* Integration with NBSAPs (National Biodiversity Strategies and Action Plans)  
* Applications for marine spatial planning, MPA effectiveness, blue carbon management  
* Decision-making use cases: budgeting, impact assessment, policy evaluation

**Global South Case Examples (5 minutes)**

* Pacific Islands: Accounts supporting fisheries and climate adaptation  
* Common implementation challenges and solutions

**Data Requirements and Sources (5 minutes)**

* Ecological data: Monitoring programs, biodiversity assessments  
* Economic data: National statistics, sectoral data, valuation studies

**13:10–13:40 | Panelist Presentations: Technical Challenges and Solutions (30 min)**

**Format:** Each panelist 7-8 minutes on specific technical domain

**Panelist 1: Spatial Data and Remote Sensing Challenges**

**Speaker:** \[Country with strong remote sensing program\]

**Content Focus:**

**Challenge 1: Cloud Cover and Image Availability**

* Problem: Tropical regions have persistent cloud cover, limiting usable satellite imagery  
* Solution: Use radar data (Sentinel-1) not affected by clouds, combine with optical when available, multi-temporal compositing  
* Tools: Google Earth Engine for automated cloud filtering and compositing  
* Result: Achieved 90% spatial coverage despite clouds  
* Lesson: Radar fills gaps but requires different processing expertise

**Challenge 2: Ground-Truthing Logistics and Costs**

* Problem: Field validation expensive, especially for remote islands or large areas  
* Solution: Stratified sampling targeting representative sites, leveraging existing monitoring programs, engaging local communities for validation points  
* Tools: Stratified sampling design using GIS, mobile apps for field data collection (e.g., ODK, Survey123)  
* Result: Reduced field costs by 60% while maintaining statistical validity  
* Lesson: Smart sampling design is force multiplier

**Challenge 3: Change Detection Accuracy**

* Problem: Distinguishing real ecosystem change from seasonal variation or image artifacts  
* Solution: Multi-year baselines establish natural variability, consistent imagery dates (same season), automated change detection with manual verification  
* Tools: CODED algorithm for change detection, visual verification protocol  
* Result: Detected genuine losses/gains while filtering false positives  
* Lesson: Automation helps but human verification still necessary

**Panelist 2: Ecological and Condition Data Challenges**

**Speaker:** \[Country with monitoring programs\]

**Content Focus:**

**Challenge 1: Indicator Selection and Data Availability**

* Problem: Scientifically ideal indicators often lack data, available data doesn't capture condition well  
* Solution: Tiered approach \- use available indicators initially, prioritize gap-filling for most policy-relevant missing indicators  
* Example: Coral health \- ideal is detailed species surveys, reality is cover and bleaching data available  
* Result: Initial accounts with basic indicators, expanded over time as monitoring improves  
* Lesson: Don't let perfect be enemy of good \- use what you have and iterate

**Challenge 2: Long-Term Monitoring Costs**

* Problem: Ecosystem condition monitoring requires sustained long-term effort, expensive to maintain  
* Solution: Tiered monitoring (intensive at key sites, extensive across region), citizen science for some indicators, integration with existing programs (fisheries surveys piggyback reef assessments)  
* Result: Maintained monitoring at reasonable cost by diversifying approaches  
* Lesson: No single monitoring approach \- combine professional, citizen, and opportunistic data

**Challenge 3: Data Quality Assurance Across Multiple Sources**

* Problem: Integrating data from research projects, government monitoring, NGO initiatives \- different methods, quality levels  
* Solution: Established minimum quality standards, metadata requirements, expert review process  
* Tools: Quality flags in database (high/medium/low confidence), transparent documentation  
* Result: Usable integrated dataset with known limitations  
* Lesson: Transparency about data quality more important than excluding imperfect data

**Panelist 3: Economic Data and Valuation Challenges**

**Speaker:** \[Country with valuation experience or economist\]

**Content Focus:**

**Challenge 1: Non-Market Valuation and Stakeholder Acceptance**

* Problem: Ecosystem services like coastal protection and cultural values lack market prices, valuation methods disputed  
* Solution: Used multiple methods (replacement cost for protection, travel cost for recreation), transparent documentation, stakeholder workshops to explain and validate  
* Result: Values accepted by policy makers when methods explained and conservative assumptions used  
* Lesson: Transparency and multiple methods build confidence, perfect estimates not required

**Challenge 2: Capturing Informal Sector Economic Activity**

* Problem: Ocean economy accounts miss substantial informal fishing, tourism, trade  
* Solution: Household surveys in fishing communities, rapid assessments at landing sites, informant interviews  
* Example: Small-scale fisheries \- official statistics captured \<30% of actual catch, surveys revealed full extent  
* Result: More accurate ocean economy accounts showing true dependence  
* Lesson: Official statistics alone inadequate \- primary data collection essential for informal sectors

**Challenge 3: Linking Biophysical and Economic Data**

* Problem: Biophysical data (hectares, fish stocks) and economic data (revenues, employment) collected separately, hard to link  
* Solution: Spatial matching (economic data georeferenced), attribution models (e.g., % of tourism revenue linked to coral reefs based on visitor surveys), expert elicitation when data lacking  
* Result: Credible linkages even with imperfect data, documented assumptions  
* Lesson: Perfect attribution impossible, reasonable approximations with transparent assumptions sufficient for policy

**Panelist 4: Data Integration and Management**

**Speaker:** \[Country with database platform experience\]

**Content Focus:**

**Challenge 1: Integrating Data from Multiple Agencies**

* Problem: Data scattered across agencies (environment, fisheries, statistics, tourism), different formats, restricted access  
* Solution: Data sharing agreements negotiated at high level, common data standards developed, shared platform built  
* Technical approach: PostgreSQL database with PostGIS for spatial data, web portal for access  
* Result: Integrated ocean accounts database accessible to all agencies  
* Lesson: Technical solutions easy, institutional coordination hard \- need high-level mandate

**Challenge 2: Metadata and Documentation**

* Problem: Data received without adequate documentation of sources, methods, limitations  
* Solution: Mandatory metadata templates, data not accepted without documentation, training for data providers  
* Standard: ISO 19115 for spatial data, custom template for tabular data  
* Result: Well-documented datasets enabling proper use and interpretation  
* Lesson: Enforce metadata standards from start, retrofitting documentation nearly impossible

**Challenge 3: Version Control and Updates**

* Problem: Data updated irregularly, users unsure which version is current, accounts become outdated  
* Solution: Database versioning system, automated alerts when new data available, scheduled account updates  
* Result: Current accounts maintained, users confident in data currency  
* Lesson: Plan for updates from beginning, not afterthought

**Panel 2: Blue Carbon and Nature Based Solutions (45 minutes)**

**Panel Composition (2-3 experts):**

* Policy expert

**Key Discussion Questions:**

* How do extent and services accounts quantify blue carbon stocks and sequestration in mangroves, seagrass, and coastal wetlands?  
* How can ocean accounting support NDC reporting, climate finance access, and nature-based solution project design?  
* What are the methodological challenges in establishing baselines, permanence, and additionality for blue carbon projects?  
* How do nature-based solutions for climate adaptation interact with ocean accounting frameworks and what opportunities exist for regional collaboration?

**Panel Presentation Content:**

* **Blue Carbon Accounting Methodology:**  
  * Extent accounts track carbon-rich ecosystems (mangroves, seagrass, salt marshes); condition accounts measure degradation and recovery; services accounts quantify carbon sequestration and storage for NDC reporting  
  * Ecosystem services accounts link blue carbon stocks to climate finance mechanisms including GCF, voluntary carbon markets, and results-based payments  
  * Nature-based solutions framework integrates blue carbon with broader coastal adaptation measures including mangrove restoration and seagrass meadow protection  
  * Example: Indonesia blue carbon accounts support NDC targets and climate finance access; Laamu Atoll social accounts document community benefits and nature-based coastal protection  
* **Methodological Challenges in Blue Carbon:**  
  * Establishing robust baselines, quantifying permanence and additionality, managing data availability in data-sparse developing country contexts  
  * Harmonizing blue carbon accounting with IPCC wetlands supplement and SEEA-EA methodologies for credible national reporting  
  * Using remote sensing and satellite data to overcome field monitoring constraints for national-scale blue carbon extent tracking  
  * Regional collaboration opportunities: joint blue carbon baseline data collection, shared methodologies, and multi-country NDC reporting frameworks  
* **Finance and Investment Pathways:**  
  * How blue carbon accounts create the evidence base for accessing GCF, Green Climate Fund, Adaptation Fund, and bilateral climate finance  
  * Linking blue economy financing facilities to nature-based solutions project pipelines supported by ocean account data  
  * Triangular cooperation opportunities: countries with advanced blue carbon accounts supporting peers in accessing and deploying climate finance  
  * Example: Indonesia blue carbon accounts supporting NDC targets and climate finance access serves as replicable model for GOAP partner countries  
* **Community and Social Dimensions of Blue Carbon:**  
  * Social accounts capture distributional impacts of blue carbon projects on coastal communities, including benefits and trade-offs  
  * Free, prior and informed consent processes and community co-design ensure blue carbon interventions align with local needs and rights  
  * Integrating traditional and indigenous knowledge into blue carbon baseline assessments to improve accuracy and community acceptance  
  * Example: Laamu Atoll Social Accounts document community dependency on healthy coastal ecosystems, strengthening the case for blue carbon investment in SIDS
