# Household Disaggregation: The Bridge Between Economy and Social Wellbeing

The household is the statistical unit that appears in both SNA economic accounts and social accounts. It is the point where ecosystem service flows become tangible human outcomes. This document establishes how household disaggregation connects the economy to each social wellbeing dimension.

## Why households are the bridge unit

In the SNA, the household sector is one of five institutional sectors (alongside non-financial corporations, financial corporations, government, and non-profit institutions serving households). Households are both:

- **Economic units** that earn income, consume products, and accumulate assets (recorded in SNA)
- **Social units** where wellbeing is experienced, food is consumed, care is provided, cultural practices are maintained, and vulnerability is felt (recorded in social accounts)

The 2025 SNA explicitly supports this bridge role. Chapter 16 (section 7.31) introduces household distributional accounts with standard breakdowns by income decile, wealth quintile, main source of income, and household type. Chapters 34 and 35 extend this to wellbeing and sustainability measurement.

For ocean accounting, the household is where:
- Fish catch becomes food security (provisioning service to consumption)
- Employment becomes material wellbeing (wages to household income)
- Ecosystem degradation becomes vulnerability (reef decline to livelihood risk)
- Cultural connection is maintained (traditional practices happen at household and community level)
- Unpaid labour enables ocean work (childcare, food preparation, equipment maintenance)

## Disaggregation categories

The following categories are drawn from SNA 2025 Chapter 16 recommendations and TG-3.5 guidance. Not all categories will be available in every country. The minimum viable disaggregation for a pilot social account is marked with *.

### Category 1: Income group *

| Breakdown | Definition | Source |
|---|---|---|
| Income quintile (Q1 to Q5) | Household equivalised disposable income, ranked and divided into five equal groups | Household income and expenditure survey |
| Wealth quintile | Net household wealth (assets minus liabilities), ranked | Household wealth survey, census |

**Why this matters for social accounts:** Income quintile is the primary lens for equity analysis. It reveals whether ocean economy benefits reach lower-income households or concentrate at the top. SNA 2025 recommends this as a standard breakdown.

**Connection to social dimensions:**
- Material wellbeing: income from ocean sectors by quintile
- Food security: fish consumption by quintile (do poorer households depend more on marine protein?)
- Vulnerability: exposure to ocean-related shocks by quintile

### Category 2: Location *

| Breakdown | Definition | Source |
|---|---|---|
| Coastal vs inland | Households within defined coastal zone (e.g., 10 km from shoreline or within coastal BSU) | Census, geospatial mapping |
| Urban vs rural coastal | Within coastal zone, urban or rural classification | Census |
| By accounting area/BSU | Mapped to specific Basic Spatial Units used in ecosystem accounts | Geospatial overlay |

**Why this matters:** Location determines physical access to ecosystem services. Coastal households receive direct benefits (fish, coastal protection, recreation access) that inland households do not. Spatial disaggregation also links social data to ecosystem extent and condition maps.

**Connection to social dimensions:**
- All dimensions: coastal vs inland comparison reveals ocean dependency
- Environmental quality: proximity to pollution sources, coastal erosion
- Health: exposure to marine-related health risks or benefits

### Category 3: Main source of income *

| Breakdown | Definition | Source |
|---|---|---|
| Fishing (commercial) | Primary income from capture fisheries (ISIC 0311) | Labour force survey, fisheries registry |
| Fishing (artisanal/subsistence) | Primary income from small-scale or subsistence fishing | Household survey, fisheries census |
| Aquaculture | Primary income from marine/brackish aquaculture (ISIC 0321) | Labour force survey |
| Tourism | Primary income from marine tourism industries | Labour force survey, tourism satellite account |
| Other ocean industry | Primary income from shipping, offshore energy, marine construction, etc. | Labour force survey |
| Non-ocean | Primary income not from ocean-related industries | Labour force survey |

**Why this matters:** Main income source determines the pathway through which ecosystem changes affect households. A fishing household is directly affected by fish stock changes (E9 to E5). A tourism household is affected by reef condition through visitor demand (E9 to E5 via different service). A non-ocean household may still depend on marine protein for food security (E10).

**Connection to social dimensions:**
- Material wellbeing: income vulnerability to ecosystem change differs by source
- Employment: decent work indicators vary dramatically across ocean sectors
- Food security: fishing households have direct protein access; others depend on markets

### Category 4: Gender of household head

| Breakdown | Definition | Source |
|---|---|---|
| Female-headed | Household where the reference person is female | Census, household survey |
| Male-headed | Household where the reference person is male | Census, household survey |

**Why this matters:** Women play significant but under-recognized roles in ocean economies, especially in fish processing, aquaculture, gleaning, and unpaid household labour that enables fishing. TG-3.5 and SDG 5 both require gender disaggregation.

**Connection to social dimensions:**
- Employment: gender gaps in ocean sector wages and formality
- Food security: female-headed households often more food-insecure
- Cultural: women's roles in traditional practices and knowledge transmission
- Unpaid labour: 87% of ocean-enabling unpaid labour is performed by women (estimated)

### Category 5: Employment formality

| Breakdown | Definition | Source |
|---|---|---|
| Formal employment | Workers with written contracts, social protection coverage, tax registration | Labour force survey (ICLS definitions) |
| Informal employment | Workers without formal contracts or social protection | Labour force survey |
| Own-account worker | Self-employed without employees | Labour force survey |
| Contributing family worker | Unpaid work in family enterprise | Labour force survey |

**Why this matters:** Informal workers in ocean sectors are invisible in standard economic statistics but often the most vulnerable. SNA 2025 Chapter 39 provides updated guidance on the informal economy. Social accounts need this disaggregation to assess decent work dimensions.

### Category 6: Ethnicity and Indigenous status

| Breakdown | Definition | Source |
|---|---|---|
| Indigenous peoples | Self-identified, following national classifications | Census, specific surveys |
| Ethnic groups | Following national classifications | Census |

**Why this matters:** Indigenous and local communities often have customary marine tenure, traditional ecological knowledge, and deep cultural connections to marine ecosystems. Distributional analysis without this category misses critical equity dimensions. See TG-3.6 (Traditional Knowledge Accounts).

## Connecting household disaggregation to social dimensions

The table below maps each social wellbeing dimension (from TG-3.5) to the household disaggregation categories that reveal the most about distributional patterns.

| Social dimension | Key disaggregation | What it reveals | Example indicator |
|---|---|---|---|
| Material wellbeing | Income quintile, Main source of income | Whether ocean income reaches poorest households | Ocean-derived income as % of total, by quintile |
| Employment | Formality, Gender, Main source of income | Who has decent work in ocean sectors | Informal employment rate in fishing, by gender |
| Food security | Income quintile, Location, Main source of income | Who depends on marine protein and who is food-insecure | Fish consumption (kg/capita/yr) by quintile and location |
| Health | Location, Income quintile, Main source of income | Who faces ocean-related health risks or benefits | Occupational injury rate in fishing; nutritional status of coastal children |
| Cultural/spiritual | Ethnicity/Indigenous, Location, Gender | Who maintains traditional marine practices | Hours/yr in traditional ceremonies, by community and age |
| Recreation | Income quintile, Location | Who has access to marine recreation | Recreation participation rate by quintile and coastal/inland |
| Environmental quality | Location, Income quintile | Who bears pollution and degradation burdens | Exposure to marine litter/pollution by coastal zone and quintile |
| Vulnerability | All categories | Who is most at risk from ecosystem degradation | Composite: income + location + dependency + adaptive capacity |

## Supply-use logic: How ecosystem service flows reach household groups

The critical accounting question is: when an ecosystem produces a service, which households receive it?

### Provisioning services (fish)

```
Ecosystem service supply: 61,000 kg/yr fish catch
    |
    +-- Market pathway (E9): 40,000 kg sold
    |       |
    |       +-- Commercial buyers (not household sector)
    |       +-- Household final consumption: 28,000 kg purchased
    |               |
    |               +-- Q1 (poorest): 3,200 kg (11.4%)
    |               +-- Q2: 4,800 kg (17.1%)
    |               +-- Q3: 5,600 kg (20.0%)
    |               +-- Q4: 6,400 kg (22.9%)
    |               +-- Q5 (richest): 8,000 kg (28.6%)
    |
    +-- Subsistence pathway (E10): 21,000 kg consumed by catching households
            |
            +-- Q1: 8,400 kg (40.0%)  <-- poorest households depend most on subsistence
            +-- Q2: 6,300 kg (30.0%)
            +-- Q3: 4,200 kg (20.0%)
            +-- Q4: 2,100 kg (10.0%)
            +-- Q5: 0 kg (0%)
```

This disaggregation reveals that poorer households depend disproportionately on direct subsistence access (E10), while richer households access fish through markets (E9). This has direct implications for food security policy: restricting fishing access affects Q1 and Q2 households through the non-market pathway far more than through market prices.

### Regulating services (coastal protection)

```
Ecosystem service supply: 12 km coastline protected by reef + mangrove
    |
    +-- All households in protected zone: 778 buildings
            |
            +-- By location:
            |       Coastal zone A (near mangrove): 180 buildings
            |       Coastal zone B (near reef): 420 buildings
            |       Coastal zone C (exposed): 178 buildings (reduced protection)
            |
            +-- By income quintile:
                    Q1: 210 buildings (27%)  <-- poorer households concentrated in flood zone
                    Q2: 195 buildings (25%)
                    Q3: 168 buildings (22%)
                    Q4: 120 buildings (15%)
                    Q5: 85 buildings (11%)
```

Coastal protection is a non-rival service (E10) -- all buildings in the protected zone benefit. But poorer households are disproportionately located in flood-prone coastal areas, so the distributive effect is progressive: ecosystem degradation harms Q1 and Q2 most.

### Cultural services (recreation)

```
Ecosystem service supply: 45,000 visitor-trips/yr reef recreation
    |
    +-- Tourist households (non-resident): 38,000 trips (84%)
    |       Not in local household disaggregation
    |
    +-- Resident households: 7,000 trips (16%)
            |
            +-- Q5: 2,800 trips (40%)  <-- richer households recreate more
            +-- Q4: 1,750 trips (25%)
            +-- Q3: 1,050 trips (15%)
            +-- Q2: 700 trips (10%)
            +-- Q1: 700 trips (10%)
```

Recreation access is regressive: richer households use it more. But employment from tourism (Chain A) may be progressive if tourism jobs reach lower-income households.

## Assembling the household-level social account

Bringing it together, a household-level social account for one accounting area would combine:

**From economic accounts (SNA, via E9 and E5):**
- Household income from ocean sectors, by income quintile and source
- Household consumption of ocean products, by quintile
- Employment in ocean sectors, by formality and gender

**From social accounts (beyond SNA, via E10 and E6):**
- Subsistence harvest, by quintile and location
- Unpaid household labour enabling ocean work, by gender
- Cultural practice participation, by community and age
- Governance participation, by community

**From ecosystem accounts (via E9, E10):**
- Ecosystem service flows allocated to household groups
- Ecosystem condition changes affecting each household group

## Open questions

1. **Data availability:** Income quintile disaggregation requires household survey data that can be linked to ocean sector employment. Many countries lack this linkage. What is the minimum viable household survey for a pilot?

2. **Spatial resolution:** BSU-level disaggregation is ideal but requires geocoded household data. Census data may only be available at district level. How fine-grained does the spatial overlay need to be?

3. **Double-counting across households:** If a fishing household sells fish to a non-fishing household, the fish appears as income for the seller and consumption for the buyer. Standard SNA handles this through supply-use tables. Does the social account need its own supply-use structure, or can it ride on the SNA tables?

4. **Subsistence boundary:** The 2025 SNA includes production for own final use within the production boundary, but TG-3.5 places subsistence activities in FG2 (outside SNA). Which treatment is correct for ocean accounts? This affects whether subsistence fish appears in E9 or E10.
