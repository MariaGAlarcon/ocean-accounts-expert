# Glossary of Terms

This glossary defines key terms used across the social accounts prototype. Terms are drawn from the SNA 2025, SEEA-EA, and GOAP Technical Guidance documents. Where a term has a specific meaning in this prototype that differs from or extends its standard definition, both are noted.

---

## Accounting framework terms

**Accounting identity.** A mathematical relationship that must hold within an account. For stock accounts: Opening + Additions - Reductions = Closing. For flow accounts: Total supply = Total use. An account that satisfies an accounting identity is more than a collection of indicators -- it is an integrated system where entries are mutually consistent.

**Accounting period.** The time interval over which stocks are measured (opening and closing) and flows are recorded. Typically one year, aligned with the national accounts reporting cycle.

**Basic Spatial Unit (BSU).** The smallest geographic unit used in ecosystem accounting. BSUs tile the accounting area without gaps or overlaps. Each BSU is assigned to one ecosystem type. Social data can be linked to BSUs when household records are geocoded.

**Double entry.** The principle that every flow is recorded as both a supply (who provides it) and a use (who receives it). This ensures internal consistency and enables cross-checking.

**Opening stock / Closing stock.** The value of a stock at the beginning and end of an accounting period. The difference between them is the net change, which must be explained by recorded additions and reductions.

**Supply-use table.** A table that records who supplies a flow and who uses it. In ecosystem services accounts, ecosystems supply services and economic units use them. In social accounts, households and communities supply social activities and various beneficiary groups use them.

## Domain and edge terms

**FG1 (Flow Group 1 -- Economy).** Flows within the SNA production boundary: market transactions, production, consumption, investment. Measured in both physical and monetary units.

**FG2 (Flow Group 2 -- Society).** Flows within and beyond the SNA production boundary. FG2 contains FG1 and adds unpaid household labour, volunteering, cultural practices, governance participation, and other non-market social activities.

**FG3 (Flow Group 3 -- Environment).** All flows including ecological processes: nutrient cycling, larval dispersal, carbon cycling. FG3 contains FG2.

**SG1 (Stock Group 1 -- SNA assets).** Produced and financial assets recognized in the SNA: vessels, infrastructure, licences, financial instruments.

**SG2 (Stock Group 2 -- Social assets).** SG1 plus human capital, social capital, and institutional capital. These are the stocks that social accounts measure.

**SG3 (Stock Group 3 -- Environmental assets).** SG2 plus natural capital: ecosystem assets such as coral reefs, mangroves, seagrass beds, fish stocks.

**Edge.** A directional flow between a stock group and a flow group in the 11-edge framework (GOAP TG-0.1). Each edge represents a category of interaction. The edges most relevant to social accounts are:

| Edge | From | To | Description |
|---|---|---|---|
| E5 | FG1 | SG2 | Economic activities contributing to social conditions (employment, income) |
| E6 | FG2 | SG2 | Social activities contributing to social conditions (volunteering, governance) |
| E7 | SG2 | FG1 | Social assets enabling economic flows (governance, human capital) |
| E8 | SG2 | FG2 | Social assets enabling social activities (institutions enabling participation) |
| E9 | SG3 | FG1 | Ecosystem services flowing to the economy (fish provisioning, tourism) |
| E10 | SG3 | FG2 | Ecosystem services flowing to society beyond market transactions (cultural values, coastal protection, subsistence provisioning) |

## Social account terms

**Human capital.** The knowledge, skills, health, and capabilities of the coastal population. Measured through indicators such as educational attainment, nutritional status, fish consumption per capita, life satisfaction, and healthcare access.

**Social capital.** The networks, relationships, trust, and collective resources that connect communities. Measured through indicators such as cooperation levels, trust indices, community partnerships, cooperative memberships, and conflict resolution mechanisms.

**Institutional capital.** The formal and informal rules, governance structures, and organizational systems that regulate human-ocean interactions. Measured through countable indicators such as MPA coverage, co-management agreements, fisheries management plans, compliance rates, and customary governance practices. This is the strongest category for accounting identities because most indicators are discrete and countable.

**Social activity (flow).** An action performed by households or communities that produces or maintains social conditions. Measured in the unit most relevant to the activity type: hours for time-based activities, physical quantities for resource transfers, counts for discrete events. Social activities are recorded in supply-use tables (Tables 4a and 4b).

**Social condition (stock).** A measurable state of human or community wellbeing at a point in time. Social conditions are the stocks that social activities (flows) create, maintain, or change. Recorded in opening/closing stock tables (Tables 3a, 3b, 3c).

**Condition index (CI).** A normalized indicator on a 0-to-1 scale, where 1 represents the reference level. Used in SEEA-EA condition accounts for biophysical indicators. In social accounts, CI-style normalization is recommended only for indicators with defensible, externally-defined reference levels (WHO thresholds, SDG targets). For subjective or index-based indicators, raw values are reported without normalization.

## Household and disaggregation terms

**Household.** The statistical unit that bridges economic accounts, ecosystem accounts, and social accounts. Households are economic units (earning income, consuming products), ecosystem service beneficiaries (using or benefiting from ecosystem services), and social units (where wellbeing is experienced).

**Income quintile (Q1 to Q5).** Households ranked by equivalised disposable income and divided into five equal groups. Q1 is the poorest 20%, Q5 is the richest 20%. The primary lens for equity analysis in social accounts.

**Disaggregation.** Breaking aggregate figures into subgroups to reveal distributional patterns. Key disaggregation categories for ocean social accounts: income quintile, location (coastal/inland), main source of income, gender of household head, employment formality, ethnicity/Indigenous status.

**Distributional supply-use table.** An extension of the ecosystem services supply-use table that adds a household disaggregation dimension. It shows how much of each ecosystem service reaches each household group. This is the primary operational bridge between ecosystem accounts and social accounts.

## Ecosystem and SEEA terms

**Ecosystem asset.** A contiguous area of a specific ecosystem type, characterized by its extent (area) and condition (biophysical state). Examples: a coral reef system, a mangrove forest, a seagrass meadow.

**Ecosystem extent account.** Records the area of each ecosystem type at opening and closing, with a change matrix showing additions and reductions by cause.

**Ecosystem condition account.** Records biophysical indicators of ecosystem health, normalized to reference levels as condition indices (CI, 0 to 1).

**Ecosystem service.** The contributions of ecosystems to benefits used in economic and social activities. Three categories: provisioning (fish, timber, water), regulating and maintenance (coastal protection, carbon sequestration, water purification, nursery habitat), and cultural (recreation, spiritual values, aesthetic enjoyment).

**Production boundary (SNA).** The boundary that defines which activities count as economic production. Activities inside the boundary are recorded in GDP. Activities outside it (unpaid household work, volunteering) are not. The treatment of subsistence activities at this boundary is a key classification question for social accounts (see File 07).

## Wellbeing dimensions (from TG-3.5)

**Material wellbeing.** Income, consumption, and wealth of households. Linked to ecosystem services primarily through market pathways (E9, E5).

**Employment.** Jobs, working conditions, formality, and decent work indicators in ocean-related sectors.

**Food security.** Access to adequate nutrition, with marine protein as a key component for coastal populations. Linked through both market (E9) and non-market (E10) pathways.

**Health.** Physical and mental health outcomes related to marine environments. Includes positive effects (nutrition, recreation) and negative effects (occupational injury, pollution exposure).

**Cultural and spiritual wellbeing.** Maintenance of traditional practices, knowledge systems, ceremonies, and spiritual connections to marine environments. Primarily non-market (E10, E6).

**Recreation.** Access to and participation in marine and coastal recreational activities. Mixed market and non-market pathways.

**Environmental quality.** The state of the local environment as experienced by households: water quality, air quality, coastal aesthetics, marine litter.

**Equity.** A cross-cutting dimension examining how benefits and burdens are distributed across household groups. Revealed through disaggregation analysis rather than measured as a standalone indicator.

**Vulnerability.** A cross-cutting dimension capturing exposure to ocean-related risks (ecosystem degradation, climate change, economic shocks) and adaptive capacity to respond. Depends on all pathways and all disaggregation categories.
