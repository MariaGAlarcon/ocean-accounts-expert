# Ecosystem Service to Social Wellbeing: Systematic Mapping

This document maps every major ecosystem service type to every social wellbeing dimension from TG-3.5, identifying the pathway (market or non-market), the relevant edges from the 11-edge framework, the indicator, and the household disaggregation that reveals the most about distribution.

## The mapping framework

Each cell in the matrix below answers: "Through what mechanism does this ecosystem service affect this social dimension, and how is that effect distributed across household groups?"

## Illustrative worked example: Coral reef decline and social outcomes

Scenario: Coral reef extent declines by 164 ha over accounting period due to bleaching. Condition index improves slightly (0.36 to 0.44) as surviving coral recovers.

**Ecosystem change recorded in ecosystem accounts:**
- Extent account: -164 ha coral reef
- Condition account: CI 0.36 to 0.44 (net improvement despite extent loss)

**Traced effects through social dimensions:**

| Social dimension | Service pathway | Expected effect | Indicator change | Household group most affected |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Material wellbeing | Fish provisioning (E9, E5) | Mixed: less habitat but improving condition | Fishing income: uncertain, depends on net fish stock response | Q1-Q2 fishing households |
| Employment | Recreation (E9, E5) | Slight positive: improving reef condition attracts divers | Tourism jobs: +2 to 5% if condition improvement is visible | Tourism workers, informal |
| Food security | Fish provisioning + subsistence (E9, E10) | Short-term decline from extent loss; medium-term recovery from condition improvement | Fish consumption Q1: -3% short term, +5% medium term | Q1 subsistence households |
| Health | Coastal protection (E10) | Reduced: less reef extent means less wave attenuation | Flood exposure: +12 households in unprotected zone | Q1-Q2 in coastal zone B |
| Cultural | Cultural services (E10) | Ambiguous: less reef but healthier remaining reef | Traditional fishing hours: -5% (fewer accessible grounds) | Traditional fishing communities |
| Vulnerability | Multiple pathways | Increased: extent loss reduces buffering; condition improvement provides partial offset | Composite vulnerability index: +0.03 for Q1 coastal, -0.01 for Q3+ | Q1-Q2 coastal, fishing-dependent |

This trace shows how a single ecosystem change (reef decline + condition improvement) has different and sometimes opposing effects across social dimensions and household groups. This is precisely the kind of analysis that integrated social accounts enable.

## Open questions

1. **Attribution vs. allocation:** The ecosystem service supply-use table allocates physical flows to users. But the social wellbeing effect depends on more than the physical flow -- it depends on prices, access, alternatives, and adaptive capacity. How far should the mapping go? The ecosystem accounting approach (stopping at physical allocation of service flows to user groups) is the preferred direction for this prototype. It keeps the accounts grounded in measurable physical quantities and avoids the modelling assumptions required for full impact assessment. Tracing through to wellbeing outcomes (impact assessment approach) remains valuable for policy analysis but sits outside the accounting framework proper. This question stays open, but the working assumption is that the accounts allocate physical flows and the interpretation of wellbeing effects is an analytical step built on top of the accounts.

2. **Counterfactual:** The vulnerability analysis assumes ecosystem decline causes social harm. But what if households have already adapted (diversified income, migrated)? The mapping shows potential pathways, not realized effects. Should the tables include an "adaptive capacity" modifier?

3. **Temporal lags:** Ecosystem changes and social outcomes do not align temporally. Fish stock collapse may take 2 to 5 years to fully affect household income. Coastal protection loss may not matter until the next major storm. How should the accounting period handle these lags?

4. **Indirect pathways:** Nursery habitat affects food security through fish stock recruitment, which is an intermediate service (E11) that feeds into a final service (E9). Should the mapping trace through intermediate services or only map final services to social dimensions?
