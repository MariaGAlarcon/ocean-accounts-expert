# Social Flows Tables: Supply-Use Accounts for Social Activities

These tables parallel the SEEA-EA ecosystem services supply-use format. They record social activities (flows) measured in hours per accounting period, structured as supply tables (who provides the activity) and use tables (who benefits from the activity).

The accounting identity is:

**Total supply of social activity = Total use of social activity**

For hours-based measurement: total hours supplied by provider groups = total hours used/received by beneficiary groups. This is the social equivalent of the ecosystem services supply-use identity where total service supply from ecosystems = total service use by economic units.

### Key insight: Ecosystem as beneficiary

Some social activities directly benefit ecosystems (volunteering for restoration, monitoring) or benefit the community as a collective good (governance). These activities flow "upward" from the social domain to the environmental domain (reducing E1/E2 pressures) or strengthen social capital (E6). The "Ecosystem" and "Community" columns capture this.

### Supply-use identity

For each row: Total supply (from Table 4a) = Total use (from Table 4b)

Example:
- Beach cleanup hours supplied: 800 hours (200 fishing HH female + 100 fishing HH male + 150 tourism HH female + 50 tourism HH male + 200 other coastal + 100 non-coastal)
- Beach cleanup hours used: 800 hours (allocated as 500 hours ecosystem benefit + 300 hours community benefit)

The identity holds because hours supplied equal hours used. The conceptual challenge is allocating "use" -- who benefits from a beach cleanup? The ecosystem benefits directly. Coastal households benefit indirectly through improved environmental quality. This parallels the challenge in ecosystem services of allocating coastal protection benefits.

### The critical question: Does ecosystem condition change social activity hours?

If seagrass condition declines (CI 0.62 to 0.58), do gleaning hours:
- **Decrease** (less productive beds, people stop gleaning) -- suggesting ecosystem decline reduces social activity?
- **Increase** (people spend more time to collect the same amount) -- suggesting efficiency loss?
- **Stay the same** (people shift to other beds or adapt) -- suggesting resilience?

This relationship between ecosystem condition and social activity volume is the empirical question that makes Table 4c valuable. It is the social equivalent of the condition-to-services data flow template used in SEEA-EA.

### Accounting identity

Opening hours + Additions - Reductions = Closing hours

This identity holds for hours as it holds for hectares in extent accounts. The "drivers of change" column is analogous to the "reason for change" in extent change matrices (managed expansion, natural expansion, managed reduction, natural reduction).

## Connecting flow tables to stock tables

Social flows (activities in hours) should produce observable changes in social stocks (conditions):

| Flow (from Table 4a/4b) | Expected stock change (from Table 3a/3b/3c) | Edge |
|---|---|---|
| Increase in governance participation hours | Increase in co-management agreements (institutional capital) | E6 |
| Increase in knowledge sharing hours | Increase in traditional skills count (human capital) | E6 |
| Increase in restoration volunteering hours | Improvement in ecosystem condition index | Reduces E1/E2 |
| Decrease in unpaid caring hours (fishing families) | May indicate fishing households leaving sector | E5 (reverse) |
| Increase in ocean recreation hours | May indicate improved ecosystem condition or income growth | E10 or E5 |
| Increase in beach cleanup hours | Improvement in environmental quality indicators | Reduces E2 |

This is the flow-to-stock link that makes social accounts an integrated system. If governance participation hours increase but co-management agreements do not, that signals a disconnect between social activity and institutional outcome worth investigating.

## Open questions

1. **Hours as the universal unit:** TG-3.5 proposes hours as the primary flow unit for comparability. But some activities are better measured in physical quantities (kg of fish shared, number of ceremonies) or participation counts (number of households involved). Should the supply-use tables have parallel physical and hours versions, as ecosystem services have physical and monetary versions?

2. **Joint production:** A traditional fishing trip may simultaneously be cultural practice, knowledge transfer, food provisioning, and recreation. How should the hours be allocated across categories? Options: (a) primary purpose allocation, (b) proportional split, (c) record full hours in each category and flag overlap. Option (c) breaks the supply-use identity.

3. **Valuation:** Should social activity hours be valued monetarily (e.g., at replacement cost or opportunity cost wage rates)? This would create a monetary supply-use table parallel to ecosystem services monetary accounts. TG-3.5 deliberately avoids monetization, but SNA 2025 values unpaid household work in satellite accounts.

4. **Negative flows:** Can social activities be "negative"? For example, conflict over marine resources consumes hours but reduces social capital. Should this appear as a separate row or as a "reduction" in the change account?
