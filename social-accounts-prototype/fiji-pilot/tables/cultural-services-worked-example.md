# Worked Example: Cultural Services -- Fiji

**Accounting area:** Fiji (national)
**Reference year:** 2019-20 (aligned with HIES)
**Ecosystem service:** Cultural and spiritual services (non-market)
**Pathway tested:** Non-market (E10, Chain B)

This worked example tests the cultural services pathway using available Fiji data. Unlike fish provisioning, cultural services data is sparse. The value of this example is in showing what the framework reveals even with limited data, and in identifying exactly what data collection is needed.

---

## Step 1: Ecosystem asset baseline (cultural dimension)

| Indicator | Value | Year | Source |
|---|---|---|---|
| Qoliqoli (customary marine tenure areas) | 411 registered | 2016 | iTaukei Affairs Board |
| Qoliqoli total area | ~30,011 km2 | 2016 | iTaukei Affairs Board |
| FLMMA network sites (locally managed marine areas) | ~450 | 2022 | FLMMA Network |
| Coral reef extent | ~10,020 km2 | 2022 | Allen Coral Atlas |
| Culturally significant marine sites | [no data] | -- | Not systematically mapped |

The qoliqoli system is itself a form of institutional capital (SG2). Each qoliqoli is associated with a mataqali (clan) and administered through customary governance. The 411 registered areas represent a stable institutional stock that has persisted through colonial and post-colonial periods. This makes qoliqoli comparable to MPA designations in the institutional capital account (Table 3c), but with deeper historical roots.

| Marine ceremony frequency | [no data] | -- |
| Sacred marine site access | [no data] | -- |
| Traditional boat building | [no data] | -- |

### What is not known

The HIES does not collect data on cultural marine practices. No national time-use survey exists for Fiji. Community-level ethnographic studies exist for specific sites but are not nationally representative. The critical gap is the absence of a standardized survey instrument for cultural marine activities.

## Step 4: Social stocks table (Table 3c) -- institutional capital

Institutional capital is the strongest data area for cultural services because qoliqoli registrations and FLMMA sites are countable stocks.

| Indicator | Unit | Opening (2010) | Additions | Reductions | Closing (2022) | Net change |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Registered qoliqoli areas | count | 411 | 0 | 0 | 411 | 0 |
| FLMMA network sites | count | ~250 | ~200 | 0 | ~450 | +200 |
| Co-management agreements | count | [no data] | [no data] | [no data] | [no data] | [no data] |
| Recognized traditional leaders with marine authority | count | [no data] | [no data] | [no data] | [no data] | [no data] |

**Stock identity check:** Qoliqoli: 411 + 0 - 0 = 411. Identity holds trivially (no change). FLMMA: ~250 + ~200 - 0 = ~450. Identity holds. The FLMMA growth from 250 to 450 sites over 12 years represents a measurable strengthening of institutional capital for marine governance.

## Step 5: Chain B trace (E10 pathway)

The non-market pathway for cultural services follows Chain B from File 01:

| Step | Domain | What can be measured | What cannot |
|---|---|---|---|
| Ecosystem asset (extent + condition) | SG3 | Reef extent (10,020 km2), condition indices | Culturally significant site condition |
| Ecosystem service flow (E10) | SG3 to FG2 | Qoliqoli count (411), FLMMA sites (~450) | Ceremony frequency, sacred site access, TEK hours |
| Social activity | FG2 | FLMMA meeting hours (partial) | Traditional practice hours, knowledge sharing |
| Social condition (E6) | SG2 | Institutional capital (qoliqoli, FLMMA stocks) | Cultural wellbeing index, intergenerational knowledge continuity |

**The framework functions even with missing data.** It identifies the pathway, names the indicators needed at each step, and shows where the measurement chain breaks. This is more useful than a dashboard of available indicators because it reveals the structure of what is missing.

## Step 6: What HIES data reveals about social capital

Although the HIES does not directly measure cultural services, it contains proxies for social capital that are relevant:

| HIES indicator | Value | Relevance to cultural services |
|---|---|---|
| Coping: help from friends/relatives | 62.9% of households (68.8% rural) | Measures the strength of social networks that cultural practices maintain |
| Coping: help from government | 35.5% (55.2% rural) | Shows institutional capital in action |
| Subsistence workers: poverty rate | 38.4% | Subsistence communities are cash-poor but may be culturally rich |
| Family/community workers: poverty rate | 48.6% | The most culturally active employment category is the poorest |
| Inter-HH sharing (implicit) | Subsistence income FJ$1,608/yr rural | Sharing is how subsistence production becomes community food security |

**Key insight:** The HIES poverty data reveals a paradox that only social accounts can explain. The employment categories most associated with cultural marine practices (subsistence, family/community work) have the highest poverty rates (38-49%). Standard economic accounts classify these communities as the most disadvantaged. Social accounts would additionally show their institutional capital (qoliqoli), social capital (sharing networks), and cultural capital (traditional knowledge) -- forms of wealth invisible to the SNA.

## Step 7: Data collection needed

To populate the cultural services tables, Fiji would need a community survey covering these indicators:

| Indicator | Unit | Method | Sample frame | Priority |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Traditional ceremony frequency | count/yr per community | Community leader interview | Stratified by division, urban/rural | High |
| Ceremony participation | persons per ceremony | Community leader interview | Same | High |
| Traditional ecological knowledge holders | count by age/gender | Community census module | FLMMA communities (~450 sites) | High |
| Sacred marine site count and access | count + hours/yr | Participatory mapping | Coastal tikina | Medium |
| Customary governance meeting hours | hours/yr | FLMMA network records | FLMMA sites | High (low cost) |
| Traditional fishing practice hours | hours/yr | Time diary (1 week sample) | Fishing households in coastal tikina | Medium |
| Youth participation in cultural practices | hours/yr by age | Time diary | Same | Medium |
| Intergenerational knowledge transmission | hours/yr mentor-mentee | Structured interview | Elders + youth in fishing communities | High |

**Most cost-effective starting point:** The FLMMA network already covers ~450 communities with established relationships. Adding a cultural practices module to existing FLMMA monitoring would be far cheaper than a standalone national survey. The FLMMA data collection infrastructure is an institutional asset that reduces the cost of social accounting.

## Step 8: Comparison with fish provisioning

| Dimension | Fish provisioning | Cultural services |
|---|---|---|
| Data availability | Moderate (HIES, FAO, MACBIO) | Very low (qoliqoli counts only) |
| Accounting identity testable? | Yes (supply = use, tonnes balance) | Partially (institutional capital stock identity holds) |
| Household disaggregation possible? | Yes (income quintile from HIES) | Not yet (no household-level cultural data) |
| Gender dimension visible? | Partially (gleaning is 71% female) | Not yet (but likely strongly gendered) |
| Policy relevance | High (food security, livelihoods) | High (customary tenure, governance, identity) |
| What the framework adds | Distributional supply-use table showing who gets the fish | Institutional capital account showing governance health; identifies data gaps precisely |
