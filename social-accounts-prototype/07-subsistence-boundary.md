# The Subsistence Boundary: Where Do Subsistence Activities Sit in Ocean Accounts?

This document explores a classification question that has direct consequences for how social accounts record ecosystem service flows. The 2025 SNA and the GOAP Technical Guidance (TG-3.5) treat subsistence activities differently, and the choice of treatment determines whether subsistence fish (and other subsistence harvest) appears in E9 (economy) or E10 (society beyond the economy).

---

## The problem

Subsistence fishing, gleaning, and harvesting are activities where households catch or collect marine resources for their own consumption rather than for sale. These activities are widespread in Pacific Island nations, coastal communities in Southeast Asia, the Caribbean, and parts of Africa. For many coastal households, subsistence marine harvest is the primary source of animal protein.

The classification question: is subsistence fishing an economic activity (inside the SNA production boundary) or a social activity (outside it)?

The answer matters because it determines which edge in the 11-edge framework carries the flow:

| Treatment | Edge | Domain | Implication |
|---|---|---|---|
| Subsistence fishing is economic production (own final use) | E9 (SG3 to FG1) | Economy | Recorded in SNA supply-use tables; valued at equivalent market prices; appears in GDP |
| Subsistence fishing is a social activity outside SNA | E10 (SG3 to FG2) | Society (beyond economy) | Not in SNA; recorded in social accounts only; measured in physical units (kg, hours) |

---

## What the SNA says

### SNA 2008 (current)

The 2008 SNA includes production for own final use within the production boundary (paragraph 6.32). This covers:
- Agricultural products retained by producing households
- Own-account construction
- Housing services produced by owner-occupiers

By extension, subsistence fishing (fish caught and consumed by the catching household) falls within the production boundary. The fish should be valued at equivalent market prices and recorded as both output and final consumption of the household sector.

### SNA 2025 (update)

The 2025 SNA retains this treatment and reinforces it. Chapter 7 (section 7.31) on household distributional accounts explicitly includes own-account production. The broader wellbeing framework in Chapters 34 and 35 does not change the production boundary -- it extends measurement beyond it.

Under the SNA, subsistence fish is E9: an ecosystem service flowing to the economy. The household is simultaneously the producer and consumer.

---

## What TG-3.5 says

GOAP Technical Guidance document TG-3.5 (Social Condition Accounts) places social activities in FG2 -- the broader domain that contains the economy (FG1) but extends beyond it. TG-3.5 describes activities "outside the SNA production boundary" including:
- Unpaid household labour
- Volunteering
- Customary practices
- Subsistence activities (in some interpretations)

The ambiguity arises because TG-3.5 does not explicitly contradict the SNA. It describes FG2 as containing FG1, so anything in FG1 is also in FG2. The question is whether subsistence activities are in FG1 (and therefore also in FG2) or only in FG2.

---

## The case for E9 (subsistence as economic production)

1. **SNA consistency.** The SNA production boundary is well-defined and internationally agreed. Placing subsistence fishing inside it aligns ocean accounts with national accounts.

2. **Valuation.** If subsistence fish is in E9, it can be valued at market-equivalent prices and included in GDP. This makes subsistence harvest visible in economic statistics, which is important for countries where subsistence is a large share of food supply.

3. **Supply-use integration.** The fish appears in the ecosystem services supply-use table alongside commercial catch. Total fish provisioning = commercial catch + subsistence catch. This is cleaner than splitting the same service across two edges.

4. **Precedent.** SEEA-EA ecosystem services supply-use tables already include subsistence harvest as a use by the household sector. This is standard practice in countries that compile ecosystem services accounts.

---

## The case for E10 (subsistence as social activity)

1. **Practical measurement.** Subsistence catch is rarely recorded in economic statistics. It does not pass through markets, is not taxed, and is not captured in business surveys. In practice, it is measured through household surveys or fisheries censuses -- the same instruments used for social data.

2. **Behavioral difference.** Subsistence fishing responds to different drivers than commercial fishing. It is embedded in cultural practice, community reciprocity, and food security strategies. Treating it as "production" misses these social dimensions.

3. **Policy relevance.** Many policy questions about subsistence fishing are social rather than economic: Who has customary access? How does subsistence harvest support food security for the poorest? What happens to vulnerable households when access is restricted? These questions sit in the social domain.

4. **Avoiding double counting.** If subsistence fish is in E9 (valued at market prices) AND the social account records the food security benefit, there is a risk of counting the same fish twice -- once as economic output and once as social benefit. Placing it in E10 avoids this.

---

## A possible resolution: record once, report in both

The fish is a single physical flow. It should be recorded once and allocated to one edge. The recommended approach:

1. **Record subsistence fish in E9** (consistent with SNA production boundary). This means it appears in the ecosystem services supply-use table alongside commercial catch. Value it at market-equivalent prices for the monetary table.

2. **Disaggregate by household group in the social account.** The distributional supply-use table (File 05) shows that subsistence fish is consumed by the catching household, concentrated in Q1 and Q2 income groups. This is the social dimension of the same flow.

3. **Record the social activity (fishing hours, gleaning hours) in E10/FG2.** The activity of subsistence fishing -- the time spent, the knowledge used, the community sharing -- is a social flow even if the product is economic. Table 4a records these hours. Table 4c links them to ecosystem condition.

This separation is analogous to how paid employment works: the labour flow is in FG1 (economic), but the time-use dimension (work-life balance, gender distribution of labour) is in FG2 (social). The same activity can generate both economic and social flows without double counting, as long as the physical product (kg of fish) is counted once and the activity (hours of fishing) is counted once.

## Implications for pilot accounts

| Decision | If E9 | If E10 |
|---|---|---|
| Where does subsistence catch appear in tables? | Ecosystem services supply-use table (with commercial catch) | Social flows table only |
| How is it valued? | Market-equivalent price | Physical units only (kg, hours) |
| Which household survey collects it? | Household income/expenditure survey (consumption module) | Time-use survey or fisheries census |
| GDP impact | Included (increases GDP for subsistence-heavy economies) | Excluded |
| Food security analysis | From distributional supply-use table | Directly from social account |

## Open questions

1. **Country variation.** Different countries may classify subsistence activities differently based on their statistical traditions. Should ocean accounts prescribe one approach or allow country-specific treatment with a reconciliation note?

2. **Threshold effects.** Some households sell part of their catch and consume the rest. The commercial portion is unambiguously E9. The subsistence portion could be E9 (own final use) or E10. Where is the boundary when a household sells 30% and consumes 70%?

3. **Gleaning and seaweed collection.** These activities are often entirely subsistence and predominantly female. The SNA production boundary may technically include them as own-account production, but in practice they are almost never recorded in economic statistics. Does the formal boundary matter if no country actually measures it as production?

4. **Traditional exchange.** In many Pacific communities, fish is shared or exchanged through customary systems (not market transactions). Is customary exchange inside or outside the SNA production boundary? The 2025 SNA discusses barter transactions but customary sharing may not fit neatly into barter definitions.

5. **Implications for the 11-edge framework.** If the resolution above is adopted (product in E9, activity in E10), does this require a formal modification to TG-0.1, or is it consistent with the existing edge definitions?
