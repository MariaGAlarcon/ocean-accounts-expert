# Coral Reef Condition Accounts for Belize

## Methodology report aligned with the SEEA Ecosystem Accounting framework

**Suggested citation:** Global Ocean Accounts Partnership (GOAP). 2026. Coral Reef Condition Accounts for Belize: Methodology report aligned with the SEEA Ecosystem Accounting framework. GOAP Technical Report. Belize City.

**Contributors:** Belize Fisheries Department, Healthy Reefs Initiative, Global Ocean Accounts Partnership, University of Belize Environmental Research Institute.

## 1. Executive Summary

This report presents the first coral reef condition accounts for Belize, compiled under the System of Environmental-Economic Accounting, Ecosystem Accounting (SEEA EA) framework. The accounts track six biotic condition indicators across 169 survey sites spanning the Belize Barrier Reef system, three offshore atolls, and nine marine protected areas (MPAs). The accounting period covers 2023 (opening year) to 2025 (closing year), using field data collected by the Healthy Reefs Initiative and the Belize Fisheries Department through the Atlantic and Gulf Rapid Reef Assessment (AGRRA) protocol.

A pilot account for Ambergris Caye, within the Northern Barrier Complex, served as the test case for the methodology. The same approach was then applied to all MPAs and subregions where matched survey data exists for both years.

At the national level, live coral cover declined from 15.00% to 6.63% (condition index falling from 0.30 to 0.13), fleshy macroalgae cover increased from 17.34% to 25.01%, coral recruit density dropped from 0.37 to 0.17 per m2, and reef relief decreased from 75.52 cm to 69.19 cm. The single indicator showing improvement was coral diversity, with the Shannon index rising from 1.16 to 1.39. These results point to continued degradation of Belize's coral reefs between 2023 and 2025, with live coral cover declining by 56% and recruit density by 53% relative to the opening year.

The accounts cover 109 sites in the opening year and 59 in the closing year, with full opening/closing comparisons produced for nine geographic units. Three additional areas (Turneffe Atoll, Lighthouse Reef, and South Water Caye) have opening-year snapshots only, as they were not resurveyed in 2025.

## 2. Introduction

### 2.1 Why Condition Accounts Matter for Belize

The Belize Barrier Reef Reserve System, a UNESCO World Heritage Site, underpins the country's fisheries, tourism, and coastal protection. Reef degradation translates directly into economic loss: reduced fish stocks, lower tourism revenue, and increased coastal vulnerability to storms. Despite decades of monitoring, Belize has lacked a standardized framework for comparing reef condition across sites, MPAs, and time periods in a way that connects directly to national economic and environmental statistics.

Condition accounts fill this gap. By measuring reef health using consistent indicators, normalizing values against reference benchmarks, and reporting results in a standardized table format, the SEEA EA condition account provides a tool that reef managers, economists, and policymakers can all use.

### 2.2 SEEA EA Framework

The System of Environmental-Economic Accounting, Ecosystem Accounting (SEEA EA) is a statistical framework adopted by the United Nations Statistical Commission in 2021 (United Nations et al. 2021). It provides a standardized structure for recording the extent, condition, and services of ecosystems alongside traditional economic accounts.

A condition account records the state of an ecosystem at an opening date and a closing date. For each measurable indicator, the account reports the raw measured value, a normalized condition index (scaled 0 to 1 against a reference level), standard error, and the change between the two time points. A condition index of 1.0 means the ecosystem meets or exceeds the reference condition; 0 means total degradation relative to that benchmark. This structure makes condition comparable across ecosystems, indicators, and countries.

### 2.3 Scope

This report covers a single ecosystem type: photic coral reefs, classified as M1.3 under the IUCN Global Ecosystem Typology (Keith et al. 2020). In Belize, this encompasses the barrier reef system (the second largest in the world), three offshore atolls (Glover's Reef, Turneffe, and Lighthouse Reef), and patch reefs within the coastal lagoon.

The accounting period runs from 2023 (opening year) to 2025 (closing year). The opening year data were collected by the Healthy Reefs Initiative; the closing year data by the Belize Fisheries Department. Both campaigns followed the standardized AGRRA protocol.

### 2.4 Reporting Hierarchy

The accounts use a nested geographic hierarchy that allows results to be reported at multiple scales:

## 4. Data Source

### 4.1 AGRRA Program

The Atlantic and Gulf Rapid Reef Assessment (AGRRA) is a standardized protocol for coral reef monitoring across the Caribbean (AGRRA 2024). AGRRA surveys record benthic cover using point-intercept transects, coral colony health through individual colony measurements, fish communities through visual census, and invertebrate populations through transect counts. The protocol is designed for rapid deployment by trained survey teams and produces comparable data across sites and years.

## 5. Survey Methods

### 5.1 Benthic Point-Intercept Transects

At each survey site, divers lay 10 m transects along the reef at a consistent depth. Along each transect, 100 points are sampled at regular intervals using a point-intercept method. At each point, the diver records the primary organism or substrate directly beneath the sampling point and, where two organisms share the substrate, a secondary organism. Each organism is identified using an AGRRA taxonomic code linked to genus and functional category (e.g., hard coral, fleshy macroalgae, turf algae, sponge). Algal height is also measured at selected points. Sites typically have 6 benthic transects.

### 5.2 Quadrat Recruit Surveys

Along each benthic transect, divers survey 4 quadrats (each covering a fixed area) for juvenile coral recruits. Recruits are identified to coral genus and classified by size: small recruits (2 cm or less in diameter) and large recruits (2 to 4 cm). Quadrats with no recruits are recorded as zero. This produces a count of new coral settlers per unit area, which indicates the reef's recovery capacity.

### 5.3 Coral Community Belt Transects

At selected sites, divers survey individual coral colonies along belt transects. For each colony, the diver records the species, maximum length, width, and height, and the percentage of the colony that is pale, bleached, or diseased. Disease types follow the AGRRA disease classification. These measurements allow calculation of coral species diversity and assessment of colony health.

### 5.4 Fish Visual Census and Reef Relief

Fish transects run parallel to the benthic transects. Divers record fish species, size, and abundance within a defined belt width. At each fish transect, the maximum vertical height of reef structures (reef relief, in centimeters) is recorded. This captures the structural complexity of the reef habitat available to fish and invertebrates.

## 6. Quality Assurance

All field data pass through a quality assurance procedure before indicator calculation. The procedure checks five dimensions:

**Coordinate validation.** Site coordinates are checked against the Belize reef system boundary. Latitude values must fall within 15.9 to 18.5 degrees north; longitude values within -88.99 to -87.00 degrees west. Sites outside these bounds are flagged for review.

**Taxonomic standardization.** Each benthic organism observation is recorded as a numeric code in the AGRRA platform. These codes are resolved to scientific names and assigned to functional categories (e.g., "Calcifiers :: Coral", "Algae :: Macro :: Fleshy") using the taxonomy tables in the AGRRA metadata. Coral colony observations are resolved to genus level using the CoralTaxonomy reference table, which contains 51 Caribbean coral species across 24 genera.

**Unit harmonization.** Cover values are expressed as percentages, recruit counts per unit area, reef relief in centimeters, temperature in degrees Celsius, and depth in meters.

**Range checks.** Numeric values are checked against expected bounds: depth 0 to 20 m, water temperature 25 to 35 C, transect numbers 1 to 6, relief 0 to 200 cm. Values outside these ranges are flagged.

**Completeness assessment.** Each dataset is checked for required fields. Missing or null values are counted per field and reported. The quality assurance step produces a validation report listing all checks with pass/fail status and counts of flagged records.

## 7. Condition Indicators

### 7.1 Indicator Summary

Six biotic condition indicators were selected based on their ecological relevance, availability in the AGRRA dataset, and alignment with the SEEA EA coral reef condition framework.

| Indicator | What it measures | Ecological role | Direction | Unit | Reference level |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Live coral cover | Proportion of substrate occupied by living hard coral | Reef-building foundation | Higher is better | % | 50% |
| Fleshy macroalgae cover | Proportion of substrate occupied by fleshy macroalgae | Competitive threat to coral; nutrient loading indicator | Higher is worse | % | 50% |
| Coral recruit density | Juvenile corals per unit area | Reef recovery capacity | Higher is better | per m2 | 15 |
| Coral diversity (Shannon H') | Species diversity of coral assemblage | Community resilience | Higher is better | index | 2.0 |
| Reef relief | Maximum vertical height of reef structures | Structural habitat complexity | Higher is better | cm | 150 |
| Coral bleaching prevalence | Proportion of colonies with bleaching or paling | Thermal stress indicator; early warning of mass mortality | Higher is worse | % | 100% |

The AGRRA dataset contains additional variables beyond these six indicators. Fish biomass and abundance, recorded through visual census, are standard SEEA EA condition indicators and serve as direct inputs to fisheries provisioning and reef recreation service accounts. Dead coral cover tracks recent mortality and would complement the live coral cover indicator. Crustose coralline algae cover indicates substrate consolidation capacity. Coral disease prevalence records chronic stressors distinct from thermal bleaching. Fish biomass and bleaching prevalence should be prioritized for the next accounting period because they connect directly to ecosystem service valuations: fish biomass feeds fisheries production and tourism attractiveness estimates, and bleaching prevalence responds to climate pressures that affect all reef-derived services.

### 7.2 Calculation Methods

#### 7.2.1 Live Coral Cover

Live coral cover is calculated from the benthic point-intercept data in four steps. First, each observation point is classified by matching its primary and secondary organism codes to the AGRRA taxonomy. Organisms in the "Calcifiers :: Coral" category count as live coral. Second, coral presence is scored using a co-dominance weighting: a point with coral as both primary and secondary (or coral as primary with no secondary) scores 1.0; a point where only one of primary/secondary is coral scores 0.5; a point with no coral scores 0. Third, transect-level cover is calculated as 100 times the sum of coral presence scores divided by the total number of points on the transect. Fourth, site-level cover is the arithmetic mean of all transect values at that site.

#### 7.2.2 Fleshy Macroalgae Cover

Fleshy macroalgae cover follows the same method as live coral cover, except the functional category is "Algae :: Macro :: Fleshy" (genera such as Dictyota, Lobophora, Sargassum, and Caulerpa). The same co-dominance weighting and aggregation steps apply.

#### 7.2.3 Coral Recruit Density

Recruit density is calculated from the quadrat survey data. Along each transect, 4 quadrats are surveyed for juvenile corals. Recruits are counted and classified as small (2 cm or less) or large (2 to 4 cm). Missing quadrats are filled with zero counts to avoid inflating density by excluding empty quadrats. Total recruits across all quadrats and transects at a site are summed and divided by the total surveyed area (25 m2) to produce a density value in recruits per m2.

#### 7.2.4 Coral Diversity

Species diversity is calculated from the coral community belt transect data. Within each transect, the number of colonies of each coral species is tallied. The relative abundance of each species is p_i = (count of species i) / (total colonies). The Shannon diversity index is H' = -sum(p_i x ln(p_i)). Species richness (count of distinct species) is also recorded. Both metrics are averaged across transects at each site.

#### 7.2.5 Reef Relief

Maximum reef relief is recorded at each fish transect in centimeters. Site-level relief is the arithmetic mean of the maximum relief values across all transects at that site.

#### 7.2.6 Coral Bleaching Prevalence

Bleaching prevalence is calculated from the coral community belt transect data. Each colony record includes the percentage of the colony that is bleached and the percentage that is pale. A colony is classified as showing bleaching signs if either value exceeds zero. Site-level prevalence is the proportion of assessed colonies showing any bleaching signs, expressed as a percentage. This indicator was added to the pipeline after the initial five indicators were established; results will be included in the account tables once the calculation has been validated against the full AGRRA dataset.

### 7.3 Reference Levels

Each indicator value is normalized to a condition index between 0 and 1 by comparison against a reference level. The reference levels used here are Type 2 (regional published benchmarks) within the SEEA EA framework.

| Indicator | Reference level | Source |
|---|---|---|
| Live coral cover | 50% | Caribbean pre-1980s baseline; Gardner et al. 2003, Roff et al. 2018 |
| Fleshy macroalgae cover | 50% | Algal dominance threshold; Bruno et al. 2009 |
| Coral recruit density | 15 per m2 | Healthy Caribbean reef observations; Bak and Engel 1979 |
| Coral diversity (H') | 2.0 | Upper range of Caribbean Shannon index at healthy sites |
| Reef relief | 150 cm | Empirical 90th-percentile from Belize AGRRA dataset |
| Coral bleaching prevalence | 100% | Theoretical maximum; CI directly represents the proportion of the reef not showing bleaching |

The reef relief reference level is derived from the empirical distribution of the current dataset rather than from published literature. Within the SEEA EA reference level typology, it is closer to a Type 1 (ad-hoc) reference than the Type 2 (regional benchmark) references used for coral cover, macroalgae, and recruit density. This reference should be treated as provisional and carries more uncertainty than the literature-based values.

### 7.4 Normalization

For indicators where higher values indicate better condition (coral cover, recruit density, diversity, relief):

Condition Index = min(Measured Value / Reference Level, 1.0)

For fleshy macroalgae cover and coral bleaching prevalence, where higher values indicate degradation:

Condition Index = max(1 - (Measured Value / Reference Level), 0.0)

Measurement uncertainty is propagated to the condition index as SE_CI = SE_measured / Reference Level. All condition indices are capped at the range 0 to 1.

## 10. Data Quality and Limitations

### 10.1 Tiered Assessment

Following the SEEA EA tiered assessment framework, this condition account was evaluated across three dimensions:

| Dimension | Tier | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Feasibility | Tier 2 | 2 to 3 analysts; standard R computing environment; data provided by national agency |
| Accuracy | Tier 1 to 2 | Regional Caribbean benchmarks for reference levels (not locally calibrated); 169 sites but unequal coverage between years |
| Difficulty | Tier 1 to 2 | Standard statistical methods; data from two agencies; no advanced modelling required |

The binding constraint on accuracy is the reference level sub-procedure. The reference levels for coral cover and macroalgae rest on peer-reviewed Caribbean-wide studies with broad support. The reference levels for recruit density and coral diversity rest on weaker published consensus and carry more uncertainty.

### 10.2 Limitations

| Issue | Impact | Mitigation |
|---|---|---|
| Different survey teams in 2023 and 2025 | Potential observer bias between opening and closing years | Both teams followed standardized AGRRA protocol, which structures observations to reduce inter-observer variability |
| Not all sites resurveyed in 2025 | Turneffe Atoll and Lighthouse Reef lack closing-year data; reduces matched site pairs for change detection | Report only matched sites for opening/closing accounts; present single-year snapshots for unmatched areas |
| No MPA field in AGRRA metadata | Sites assigned to MPAs by coordinate and name matching rather than field-recorded attribution | Document assignment logic; verify with Belize Fisheries Department |
| Coral community transects absent from most subregions in 2023 | Shannon diversity and recruit density limited to closing year at most sites; prevents opening/closing comparison for these indicators | Report as "Closing only" where opening data is missing; include in future survey protocols |
| Recruit density reference level weakly established | Published Caribbean consensus for this indicator is weaker than for coral cover or macroalgae | Flag uncertainty in results; interpret recruit density condition indices with caution |
| Reef relief measured in cm, historical reference uses rugosity index | Units not directly comparable to published chain-tape rugosity literature | Use empirical reference from this dataset (150 cm) rather than converting; note this limits cross-study comparison |

### 10.3 Binding Constraints and Priorities

Advancing the accounts to higher accuracy tiers would require three actions, listed in order of impact. First, establish locally calibrated reference levels from long-term Belize monitoring data (10+ years) or paleoecological records, which would move the reference level procedure from Tier 2 to Tier 3. Second, ensure consistent site resurvey across all subregions in future accounting periods, particularly Turneffe Atoll and Lighthouse Reef. Third, add coral community belt transects to all future surveys to fill the gap in opening-year diversity data.

## 11. Future Integration

### 11.1 Linking Condition and Extent Accounts

The condition accounts presented here are designed to integrate with reef extent accounts within the SEEA EA framework. Current data collection efforts compile extent and condition separately. Reef extent can be derived from satellite imagery (e.g., Allen Coral Atlas, Sentinel-2 classification) and would quantify the total area of coral reef ecosystem assets in Belize. For coral reefs, extent is not expected to change as rapidly as condition: reef framework persists even as live coral declines, so significant extent losses (e.g., conversion of reef to rubble flat) occur on decadal timescales. This means that condition monitoring should receive proportionally more investment than extent monitoring for coral reef accounts, while the reverse may hold for ecosystems with faster extent dynamics (mangroves, seagrass). Linking condition to extent would enable area-weighted national condition indices and support ecosystem asset valuation.

### 11.2 Ecosystem Service Accounts

Condition indicators feed directly into ecosystem service accounts. Live coral cover and reef relief drive coastal protection service estimates (wave attenuation capacity). Fish biomass, once added as an indicator, would feed fisheries provisioning and reef recreation service accounts. Bleaching prevalence tracks climate vulnerability, which affects all reef-derived services. Establishing these linkages is a priority for future accounting cycles.

### 11.3 Composite Condition Index

The SEEA EA framework supports an optional composite condition index that aggregates individual indicators into a single score per geographic unit. This was not computed for the current accounts because the weighting scheme (how much weight each indicator receives in the composite) requires ecological justification that should involve reef scientists and stakeholders. Options include equal weighting, ecological importance weighting, and stakeholder-elicited weighting. A sensitivity analysis showing how the composite changes under different weighting schemes is recommended before adopting a composite for policy use.
