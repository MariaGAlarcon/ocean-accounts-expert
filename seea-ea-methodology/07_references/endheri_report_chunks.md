## Chunk 1

**Compiling the first Natural Capital Accounts in the Maldives: Methods report > Title Page and Introduction**

This report is titled "Compiling the first Natural Capital Accounts in the Maldives: Methods report." It was produced under the ENDhERI project — Enhancing Data for Holistic Ecosystem and Resource Indicators — which aims to demonstrate the value of natural capital in the Maldives through the compilation of ecosystem accounts aligned with the United Nations System of Environmental-Economic Accounting — Ecosystem Accounting (SEEA EA) framework. The project is a collaboration between the Maldives Marine Research Institute (MMRI), the Ministry of Climate Change, Environment and Energy, and the National Bureau of Statistics, with technical support from international partners including UNDP, UNEP, and the Global Ocean Accounts Partnership (GOAP).

The Maldives is a low-lying island nation in the Indian Ocean, highly dependent on marine ecosystems for economic prosperity, food security, and coastal protection. The country's economy is driven primarily by tourism and fisheries, both of which rely heavily on healthy coral reefs, seagrass meadows, and mangrove ecosystems. Despite this dependence, the contributions of these ecosystems to the national economy have not been systematically measured or integrated into national accounts.

Natural Capital Accounting (NCA) provides a systematic framework for measuring the stocks and flows of natural assets and the ecosystem services they provide. By compiling these accounts, decision-makers can better understand the economic value of nature and integrate environmental considerations into development planning and policy. The SEEA EA, adopted by the UN Statistical Commission in 2021 as an international statistical standard, provides the primary reference framework for this work. The ENDhERI project was initiated to address this gap by demonstrating how ocean accounts can be compiled for the Maldives context, using Laamu Atoll as a pilot area. The project aligns with the Global Ocean Accounts Partnership (GOAP) technical guidance on ocean accounting, and the document provides a comprehensive methodological framework covering the full accounting sequence from extent accounts through condition, ecosystem service, and asset accounts.

**Compiling the first Natural Capital Accounts in the Maldives: Methods report > Section 2.1–2.2: Framework Structure and SEEA EA Alignment**

The Basic Spatial Unit (BSU) is the fundamental spatial unit for accounting, defined as a 10m × 10m grid cell, each assigned to one ecosystem type. These definitions form the foundational vocabulary for the accounting framework applied throughout the report, derived from the SEEA EA standard and adapted for the marine context of the Maldives.

Section 2 presents the overarching framework for Natural Capital Accounting for the Ocean. The framework adopted for the Maldives ocean accounts is structured around three interconnected modules. Module 1 — Environmental Assets records the extent and condition of marine ecosystem types (coral reefs, seagrass meadows, and mangroves), providing the biophysical foundation by establishing what ecosystems exist, how large they are, and what state they are in. Module 2 — Flows to Economy captures the ecosystem services that flow from marine ecosystems to economic units, households, and government, recorded in both physical and monetary terms using supply and use tables, including provisioning services (wild fish harvest), regulating services (carbon sequestration, coastal protection, sediment nourishment), and cultural services (recreation and tourism). Module 3 — Flows to Environment addresses residual flows and environmental pressures affecting ecosystem condition, including nutrient loading, sedimentation, pollution, and climate change impacts such as coral bleaching from elevated sea surface temperatures.

The ocean accounts framework is explicitly aligned with the UN SEEA Ecosystem Accounting (SEEA EA) standard, adopted in March 2021. The SEEA EA organises ecosystem accounting into a coherent system of stocks and flows across four key modules: (1) ecosystem extent accounts, (2) ecosystem condition accounts, (3) ecosystem services accounts in physical and monetary terms, and (4) monetary ecosystem asset accounts. The alignment ensures international comparability and integration into the broader System of National Accounts (SNA). Each SEEA EA module corresponds directly to a component of the ocean accounts: extent accounts in Section 4 measure ecosystem area and change; condition accounts in Section 5 assess ecosystem health; ecosystem service accounts in Section 6 record physical and monetary supply and use; and asset accounts in Section 7 estimate monetary value of ecosystem stocks. Table 2.1 provides a mapping between the SEEA EA modules and the corresponding ocean account components compiled for Laamu Atoll.

Section 2.5 describes the spatial analysis framework underpinning all accounts. The framework uses a Basic Spatial Unit (BSU) approach based on a regular 10m × 10m grid. Each BSU is the fundamental unit for spatial accounting and is assigned to exactly one ecosystem type based on the dominant cover class within the cell. The spatial framework comprises three key layers: (1) the BSU grid providing the spatial backbone at 10-metre resolution; (2) the master ecosystem type layer derived from satellite image classification, assigning each BSU to coral reef, seagrass, mangrove, or other; and (3) the accounting area boundary defining the spatial extent from the shoreline to approximately 25 metres depth around Laamu Atoll.

The 25-metre depth limit was selected because it represents the practical limit for benthic habitat classification from satellite imagery and encompasses the depth range within which the target ecosystem types predominantly occur. The total accounting area for Laamu Atoll covers 884.63 km², encompassing 56 reefs and 90 islands. ## Chunk 7

**Compiling the first Natural Capital Accounts in the Maldives: Methods report > Section 4.1: Benthic Classification and Ground Truthing**

Six Marine Protected Areas (MPAs) have been established to protect critical marine habitats, with varying enforcement and management effectiveness. Figure 3.1 shows Laamu Atoll's location within the Maldives archipelago, and Figure 3.2 presents a detailed map of reef distribution and MPA boundaries.

Section 4 presents the Extent Accounts. The extent accounts require spatially explicit mapping of ecosystem types, achieved through supervised classification of satellite imagery validated against 357 field-collected ground truth points. At each point, substrate type, benthic cover, and depth were recorded using drop-camera surveys, snorkel transects, and diver observations. The classification workflow involved two parallel approaches: (1) Sentinel-2 imagery at 10-metre resolution for atoll-wide classification using Spectral Angle Mapper (SAM) supervised classification, and (2) high-resolution commercial satellite imagery (Pleiades at 0.3m, Planet SuperDove at 3m, SPOT 6/7 at 1.5m) for detailed classification using Random Forest machine learning.

The SAM algorithm was applied to atmospherically corrected and sun-glint removed Sentinel-2 Level-2A imagery. SAM determines spectral similarity between each image pixel and reference endmembers derived from ground truth data, measuring the angle between spectral vectors. It was selected for its relative insensitivity to illumination variations and albedo effects, which are important in shallow marine environments where water column effects alter spectral signatures. Classification was performed for 2017 (opening extent) and 2020 (closing extent) using the best available cloud-free composites. For selected priority areas, Random Forest classification was applied to higher-resolution imagery, constructing an ensemble of decision trees trained on random subsets of training data with final classification by majority vote. Both approaches classified into coral reef, seagrass, mangrove, sand, rubble, deep water, and land categories.

A detailed comparison for Hithadhoo island evaluated Sentinel-2 against Pleiades for mangrove mapping. High-resolution imagery substantially improved boundary delineation and detection of features such as canopy gaps, but Sentinel-2 was adequate for aggregate atoll-wide accounting purposes.

Section 5 presents the Condition Accounts. Coral reef condition was assessed through underwater visual census surveys at 17 sites distributed across Laamu Atoll. At each site, belt transects were established on the reef slope at 5–10 metres depth, with each transect covering 1,600 m² (typically 4 transects of 20m × 20m per site). Data recorded included percent cover of live hard coral by genus, soft coral, dead coral, algae categories (macroalgae, turf, crustose coralline algae), other substrates, bleaching presence and severity, fish abundance and diversity, and key invertebrates (crown-of-thorns starfish, giant clams, sea urchins). The 17 sites were selected to represent exposed outer reef slopes, sheltered inner lagoon reefs, and channel reefs, with surveys conducted during 2022 and 2023 field campaigns.

Mangrove condition was assessed at Hithadhoo through quadrat-based sampling with 10m × 10m plots along transects perpendicular to the shoreline. Within each quadrat: tree species identification, tree height using clinometer, diameter at breast height (DBH) for stems ≥2.5 cm, stem density, canopy cover using densiometer, and seedling counts for regeneration assessment. Statistical models were developed to relate field-measured condition indicators to remotely sensed variables, enabling spatial extrapolation across the full accounting area. Relationships between in-situ coral cover and satellite-derived spectral indices were explored, and seagrass biomass models used empirical relationships with water column-corrected Sentinel-2 reflectance. Generalised linear models and Random Forest regression were tested for predicting condition from remote sensing, evaluated using cross-validation and independent test datasets.

The dominant coral genera were Porites, Acropora, and Pocillopora, with turf algal cover inversely correlated with live coral cover, consistent with competitive dynamics on degraded reefs.

Section 6.2 values the fisheries nursery service provided by coral reefs and seagrass meadows — their role in supporting juvenile fish growth, survival, and recruitment to adult fisheries populations. The nursery value was estimated using the productivity change method, which quantifies the increase in adult fish biomass attributable to nursery habitat availability. The approach uses the Log Response Ratio (LRR) to compare fish density in areas with and without nursery habitat. The LRR for coral reef nursery habitat was 31%, meaning fish density in areas with coral reef nursery habitat was 31% higher than in comparable areas without. For seagrass nursery habitat, the LRR was 13%.

To estimate the nursery contribution to harvestable fish biomass, a juvenile mortality rate of 95% was assumed, reflecting high natural mortality before recruitment to the adult fishable population — meaning only 5% of enhanced juvenile production survives to harvestable size. The nursery service calculations show the estimated additional fish biomass attributable to nursery habitat, the proportion surviving to recruitment, and the resulting monetary value using market prices of landed fish. This service demonstrates a clear economic rationale for maintaining healthy coral reef and seagrass habitats as inputs to fisheries productivity.

**Compiling the first Natural Capital Accounts in the Maldives: Methods report > Section 7.1–7.2: Mangrove and Seagrass Carbon Stock Estimation**

The physical tables show service quantities supplied by each ecosystem type and used by economic sectors. The monetary tables reveal that coral reefs are the dominant supplier of ecosystem service value driven by recreational tourism (USD 30.43M/yr), seagrass provides the second-largest value dominated by carbon sequestration, and mangroves contribute the smallest absolute value due to limited extent in Laamu Atoll.

Section 7 presents the Asset Account, estimating the monetary value of ecosystem assets through carbon stock valuation. Mangrove carbon stock was estimated using allometric equations from Wartman et al. (2022), providing species-specific biomass models for Rhizophora mucronata and Ceriops tagal. For R. mucronata, above-ground biomass (AGB) was calculated as AGB = a × DBH^b × Height^c with parameters calibrated for Indo-Pacific populations. Below-ground biomass was estimated as a fixed proportion via root-to-shoot ratios. The total carbon stock in the Hithadhoo mangrove stand was estimated at 1,283.58 Mg C, including above-ground carbon, below-ground carbon, and soil organic carbon (SOC) to 1 metre depth.

Seagrass carbon stock used empirical regression models: Above-ground carbon (AGC) = 21.337810 + 0.13514 × [Coverage], estimating carbon in leaves, stems, and rhizomes above sediment; and Below-ground carbon (BGC) = 53.2301 + 0.3055 × [Coverage], estimating root and buried rhizome carbon. Below-ground carbon represents a larger proportion due to extensive root systems and organic burial. The total seagrass carbon stock across Laamu Atoll was estimated at 4,209.34 tonnes C, with per-hectare density varying across sites reflecting differences in species composition, coverage, and sediment characteristics.

The combined carbon stock for both ecosystems was approximately 503,459 Mg C. Converting to CO2 equivalent (×3.67) and applying the SCC of USD 185/tonne CO2 with a 2% discount rate for NPV, the total monetary value of the carbon asset was estimated at USD 94,443,859 (approximately USD 94.4 million), equivalent to MVR 1,454,435,426 (approximately MVR 1.45 billion). Sensitivity analysis showed high sensitivity to discount rate choice: at 1% the value increases substantially, at 3% it decreases, underscoring ethical dimensions of intergenerational equity in long-term environmental valuation.
